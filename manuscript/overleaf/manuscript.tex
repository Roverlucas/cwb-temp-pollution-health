\begin{abstract}

\textbf{Background:}\quad
Climate--health policy in Latin America predominantly targets
heatwaves, leaving the joint respiratory impacts of cold spells and
fine particulate matter ($\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$) understudied.
We aimed to quantify the delayed, non-linear compounding effects of
temperature extremes and $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ on respiratory
hospitalisations in a subtropical metropolis.

\textbf{Methods:}\quad
We conducted a time-series analysis of 42\,716 daily respiratory
hospital admissions, temperature, and $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ in Curitiba,
Brazil (2022--2024). We applied a methodological triangulation design:
$K$-means clustering to identify environmental regimes, distributed
lag non-linear models (DLNMs) to quantify exposure--response
relationships and lag structures, and machine learning (XGBoost) with
SHAP analysis to assess predictive feature importance.

\textbf{Findings:}\quad
Over 1096~days, clustering identified delayed cold--hospitalisation
correlations peaking at 3--10~days. The DLNM confirmed this pattern,
establishing a minimum morbidity temperature of
17${\cdot}$8\,°C. Extreme cold at the first percentile
(5${\cdot}$2\,°C) yielded a cumulative relative risk (RR) of
1${\cdot}$42 (95\% CI 1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84) over 0--21~days,
equating to approximately 14 excess admissions per extreme cold day.
Heat effects were not statistically significant in any stratum.
Stratified DLNMs identified pneumonia (J09--J18) as the most
cold-sensitive diagnosis (RR=2${\cdot}$08 [1${\cdot}$43--3${\cdot}$01])
and revealed that women were significantly more vulnerable to moderate
cold than men ($p$=0${\cdot}$004). While DLNMs lacked power to detect
independent $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ effects, machine learning
consistently ranked $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ as a secondary compounding
factor.

\textbf{Interpretation:}\quad
Cold extremes, rather than heat, drive the highest
temperature-related respiratory burden in this subtropical metropolis,
with pneumonia and women disproportionately affected.
The agreement of three methodologically independent approaches
confirms a practical 3-to-10-day delayed window for preventive
clinical intervention. Integrating cold-spell triggers into existing
heat-centric early-warning systems could avert considerable
respiratory morbidity.

\end{abstract}

\section*{Research in Context}

\textbf{Evidence before this study}

We searched PubMed and Scopus (Jan 1, 2010, to Dec 31, 2024), with
no language restrictions, using the terms `temperature extremes',
`hospital admissions', `distributed lag', and `Southern
Hemisphere' or `Latin America'. Globally, cold-attributable
mortality exceeds heat impacts, yet subtropical South American cities
remain unrepresented, making regional morbidity evidence scarce.
Existing temperature--morbidity analyses rely on correlation-based
approaches unable to disentangle delayed effects from shared
seasonality, limiting evidence quality. No prior study applied
methodological triangulation (clustering, DLNM, machine learning) to
assess the joint, delayed effects of cold extremes and
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ on respiratory morbidity in this region.

\textbf{Added value of this study}

To our knowledge, this is the first study to evaluate the compounding
role of temperature extremes and air pollution on respiratory
hospitalisations in a subtropical South American city. We quantified a
42\% excess risk of respiratory admissions during extreme cold (1st
percentile of $T_\mathrm{min}$, 5${\cdot}$2\,°C;
RR=1${\cdot}$42 [1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84]), while heat effects
were non-significant across all age, sex, and diagnostic strata.
Stratified DLNMs identified pneumonia as the most cold-sensitive
diagnosis (RR=2${\cdot}$08) and revealed that women are significantly
more vulnerable to moderate cold than men ($p$=0${\cdot}$004). We
identified a 3--10~day delayed window between cold exposure and
the hospitalisation peak. The consistency of our independent
frameworks (inferential, exploratory, and predictive) indicates this
lag is a reliable physiological signal, challenging the heat-centric
focus of Latin American public health policy.

\textbf{Implications of all the available evidence}

Combined with global evidence that cold-attributable mortality exceeds
heat impacts, our findings indicate that heat-centric early-warning
systems in subtropical Latin America, like Brazil's VIGIAR-SUS,
overlook a large respiratory burden. For policy and practice,
surveillance systems must integrate joint cold-spell and
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ forecasts, with sex-targeted outreach given
women's higher vulnerability. The 3-to-10-day delay provides an
operational window for primary care tele-monitoring and emergency
stockpiling. Future research should prioritise multi-city pooled
analyses across the subtropical Southern Cone.

\section{Introduction}

Growing evidence shows that cold-attributable mortality
exceeds heat-attributable mortality in most
climates,\cite{Gasparrini2015} yet climate--health policy remains
overwhelmingly focused on heat alerts and heatwave preparedness.
This asymmetry is particularly pronounced in subtropical cities of
Latin America, where winter temperatures can fall below
5\,°C but early-warning systems rarely include cold-spell
triggers.\cite{Bell2024,McMichael2006}

Low ambient temperatures suppress mucociliary clearance, impair
alveolar macrophage function, and increase indoor crowding, favouring
respiratory pathogen transmission.\cite{Linares2025} Unlike
heat-related morbidity, which manifests within 0--3~days, cold effects
exhibit delayed lags of 3--14~days, complicating detection in routine
surveillance.\cite{Ebi2021,Cheng2024} Concurrently,
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ induces systemic inflammation and oxidative stress,
compounding cold-induced mucosal vulnerability.\cite{Brook2010}
Despite improvements in air quality
globally,\cite{Yu2025,Gopikrishnan2025} extreme pollution episodes
persist and disproportionately affect vulnerable
groups.\cite{Hertzog2024,Requia2024,Nashwan2024}

Despite these known mechanisms, formal inferential evidence on the
temperature--morbidity relationship remains scarce for subtropical
South America. The landmark 384-location study by Gasparrini
et~al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} did not include any subtropical Southern
Brazilian city. More recently, the Lancet Countdown South America
reports have documented rising heat-related mortality and wildfire
exposure across the region,\cite{LancetCountdownSA2025} yet
cold-attributable morbidity remains largely unaddressed in these
assessments. Multi-city studies in Brazil have focused on air
pollution and mortality,\cite{Requia2024} while temperature--morbidity
analyses have relied on correlation-based approaches that cannot
disentangle delayed effects from shared seasonality. As a result,
a key question remains unanswered for this region: does cold
or heat impose the greater respiratory hospitalisation burden when
combined with high air pollution episodes, and over what time horizon?

Within the Compound Climate Extremes
framework,\cite{Zscheischler2020} we address this gap through a
methodological triangulation applied to 42\,716~respiratory hospital
admissions, daily temperature, and EPA-corrected $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ in
Curitiba, the largest subtropical city in southern Brazil. Our primary
objective is to quantify the non-linear, delayed exposure--response
relationship between temperature extremes and respiratory admissions.
The triangulation proceeds in three stages: unsupervised clustering
identifies environmental regimes and lagged associations without
parametric assumptions; DLNMs quantify the exposure--response relationship; and gradient-boosted trees with SHAP analysis offer
independent predictive corroboration.

Curitiba serves as a proxy for densely populated metropolitan areas
in the subtropical Southern Cone (Southern Brazil, Northern Argentina,
Uruguay, Chile), which face rapid urbanisation and housing insulation
deficits that may render populations paradoxically vulnerable to
moderate cold spells --- a `hidden vulnerability' overlooked in
Northern Hemisphere-centric
literature.\cite{Gasparrini2015,Cohen2017}

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Study design and setting}

We conducted an ecological time-series study of daily environmental
exposures and respiratory hospital admissions in Curitiba, Paraná,
Brazil (population~$\sim$1${\cdot}$8~million) from
1~January~2022 to 31~December~2024 (1096~days). Curitiba is the
largest city in southern Brazil with a Köppen Cfb (subtropical)
climate, winter minima below 5\,°C, and summer maxima approaching
30\,°C --- making it representative of the broader subtropical belt
of southern South America. The study-period $T_\mathrm{min}$
distribution (mean 14${\cdot}$8\,°C, P10=9${\cdot}$8\,°C) was
comparable to the 1961--2024 INMET climatology (mean 13${\cdot}$3\,°C,
P10=7${\cdot}$8\,°C; appendix figure~9), indicating that the 2022--2024
period did not experience a regime shift in cold extremes.

\subsection{Data sources}

Individual-level hospital admission records were obtained from the
Brazilian Unified Health System (SIH/SUS--DATASUS) for 2022--2024
($N$=42\,716 respiratory admissions, ICD-10 J00--J99). The primary
outcome was the aggregate daily count of all-cause respiratory
admissions (51\% male; 38\% aged $\geq$65~years).

Daily minimum ($T_\mathrm{min}$), mean ($T_\mathrm{med}$), and
maximum ($T_\mathrm{max}$) temperatures, relative humidity, and
atmospheric pressure were obtained from INMET automatic station~A807.
$T_\mathrm{min}$ served as the primary exposure in the DLNM; all six
meteorological and pollution variables were used in the clustering and
machine learning analyses. For Expert Team on Climate Change Detection
and Indices (ETCCDI) percentile calculations, the full record
(1961--2024) was used; combined analyses were restricted to 2022--2024.

$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ concentrations were measured using a single PurpleAir
Flex-II sensor (Sensor ID~90875) at daily resolution. Although
relying on a single monitoring site, a recent spatialisation study
using a nine-sensor network in Curitiba empirically demonstrated
that daily $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ temporal fluctuations at the central
district are highly correlated ($0{\cdot}76 < r < 0{\cdot}93$) with
the entire metropolitan grid, validating the use of a central sensor
as a reliable temporal proxy for city-wide exposure in ecological
time-series analyses.\cite{daCosta2024} Raw readings were corrected
using the EPA nationwide correction equation:\cite{Barkjohn2021}
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}^{\mathrm{corr}} =
    0.524 \times \mathrm{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}^{\mathrm{sensor}}
    - 0.0862 \times \mathrm{RH} + 5.75
  \label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
%
This correction is validated against Federal Equivalent Method
monitors.\cite{Holder2020,Magi2020,Barkjohn2022} An inter-channel
QC protocol excluded days when differences exceeded 5~µg/m$^3$ and
70\% relative difference simultaneously, yielding 697~valid days
(63${\cdot}$6\%). The 36${\cdot}$4\% missingness was not MCAR,
occurring more frequently on colder days, limiting inferential power
(appendix pp~2--5). An independent validation against
CAMS/Copernicus reanalysis data (Open-Meteo Air Quality API) over
568~overlapping days confirmed strong temporal agreement with the
EPA-corrected series (Pearson $r$=0${\cdot}$73, Spearman
$\rho$=0${\cdot}$76, $p$$<$0${\cdot}$001), and a sensitivity DLNM
using CAMS as exposure ($N$=882~days) reproduced the null
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ finding (P90 RR=1${\cdot}$19
[0${\cdot}$66--2${\cdot}$15]), supporting the reliability of the
sensor-based assessment.

\subsection{Definitions and missing data}

Extreme temperature days were defined using ETCCDI monthly percentiles
(TX90p for extremely hot, TN10p for extremely cold) over the 1961--2024
baseline. Heatwaves: $\geq$3~consecutive extreme hot days; cold spells:
$\geq$3~consecutive extreme cold days. Missing data rates:
$T_\mathrm{min}$~2${\cdot}$6\%, $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$~36${\cdot}$4\%,
hospitalisations~0${\cdot}$2\%. Primary analyses used complete cases;
sensitivity analyses with imputation produced similar results
(Supplement).

\subsection{Clustering analysis (structural-exploratory evidence)}

As the first analytical step, K-means
clustering\cite{MacQueen1967} was applied to six standardised variables
($T_\mathrm{max}$, $T_\mathrm{med}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$,
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$, relative humidity, atmospheric pressure) on
complete-case days, with optimal $K$ selected by silhouette
score.\cite{Rousseeuw1987} Lagged cross-correlation functions (lags
0--28~days) were computed between cluster membership and daily
hospitalisations to identify temporal associations without
parametric constraints. PCA was performed on standardised
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$, and hospitalisations ($N$=683).
This provides an independent, assumption-free exploration of the
lag structure (appendix pp~5--7).

\subsection{Distributed lag non-linear model (inferential evidence)}
\label{sec:dlnm}

Guided by the lag patterns identified in the clustering analysis,
distributed lag non-linear models
(DLNM)\cite{Gasparrini2010,Gasparrini2011} were applied to formally
quantify the non-linear exposure--response relationship and its
delayed lag structure:
%
\begin{equation}
  \log\!\big(\mathrm{E}[Y_t]\big) = \alpha
    + \mathbf{s}\bigl(x_t, \mathrm{lag}; \boldsymbol{\eta}\bigr)
    + \sum_{d=1}^{6} \beta_d \,\mathrm{DOW}_{d,t}
    + \gamma \,\mathrm{Holiday}_t
    + \mathrm{ns}(\mathrm{time}_t,\, 7\,\mathrm{df/year})
  \label{eq:dlnm}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_t$ is the daily count of respiratory admissions (quasi-Poisson),
$\mathbf{s}(\cdot)$ is the cross-basis function (natural cubic splines:
exposure df=4, lag df=5), DOW denotes day-of-week indicators, and
$\mathrm{ns}(\mathrm{time}, 21\,\mathrm{df})$ captures trend and
seasonality. Maximum lag was set at 21~days, consistent with the
standard adopted in multi-country temperature--mortality
analyses\cite{Gasparrini2015} and reflecting the known 3--14-day
latency of cold-related respiratory
effects.\cite{Linares2025,Cheng2024} The minimum morbidity temperature
(MMT) --- defined as the temperature at which the cumulative
exposure--response curve reaches its minimum risk, analogous to the
minimum mortality temperature of Gasparrini
et~al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} --- was identified by grid search.
Confidence intervals were computed via the delta method. Sensitivity
analyses varied the maximum lag (14, 21, 28~days), seasonal df
(6, 7, 8~df/year), and excluded 2022 to assess residual COVID-19
effects. Time-series were also
decomposed using STL, and ADF tests assessed stationarity.

\subsection{Machine learning (predictive convergence evidence)}

Machine learning models (XGBoost,\cite{Chen2016}
MLP,\cite{Goodfellow2016} ELM\cite{Huang2006}) were trained to
forecast daily hospitalisations at horizons of 0--7~days using a
70/30 temporal split. Performance was evaluated using MSE, RMSE, MAE,
and MAPE. SHAP values\cite{Lundberg2017} quantified feature
importance; agreement between SHAP rankings and DLNM findings
provides independent methodological corroboration (appendix pp~7--9).

\paragraph{Exploratory scenario simulations}
ML models were applied to CMIP6 SSP5-8${\cdot}$5 projections
(EC-Earth3-Veg-LR, $\sim$100~km) for Curitiba, generating
hospitalisation scenarios for 2050. Monte Carlo simulations
superimposed synthetic cold waves and wildfire $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$
episodes (70--100~µg/m$^3$) onto the 2050 baseline to quantify
compound extreme-event burdens.

\paragraph{Ethics and reporting}
Because this study relied exclusively on de-identified, publicly
available aggregated population-level data from DATASUS, INMET, and
PurpleAir, the requirement for Institutional Review Board (Ethics
Committee) approval and individual informed patient consent was waived
under the Brazilian National Health Council Resolution~510/2016.
Reporting follows the STROBE guidelines for observational
studies.\cite{VonElm2007}

\subsection{Role of the funding source}
The funder of the study had no role in study design, data collection,
data analysis, data interpretation, or writing of the report.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Descriptive statistics}

Over 1096~study days, we recorded 42\,716~respiratory admissions and
3715~in-hospital deaths (case fatality 8${\cdot}$7\%; mean
32${\cdot}$3/day, SD~11${\cdot}$7; Table~\ref{tab:descriptive}).
EPA-corrected $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ was low (median~4${\cdot}$12~µg/m$^3$;
4${\cdot}$7\% of days exceeding the WHO guideline). ETCCDI percentiles
identified 215~extremely hot days (20${\cdot}$1\%) and 39~extremely
cold days (3${\cdot}$7\%), aggregating into 25~heatwaves but only
4~cold-spell events (appendix table~1). Despite their low frequency, cold
events were the only ones associated with significant excess morbidity
(§\ref{sec:dlnm_results}).

\paragraph{Age-stratified descriptive analysis}
Among 35\,275 admissions with valid age data (82${\cdot}$6\% of
total), age-group decomposition revealed distinct cold-sensitivity
patterns (appendix table~13). Children aged $<$5~years
accounted for 21${\cdot}$4\% of admissions ($n$=7540; mean
6${\cdot}$9/day), adults 5--64~years for 45${\cdot}$9\%
($n$=16\,186; 14${\cdot}$8/day), and adults $\geq$65~years for
32${\cdot}$7\% ($n$=11\,549; 10${\cdot}$5/day). In-hospital
mortality was strongly age-dependent: 0${\cdot}$3\% in children,
4${\cdot}$6\% in adults, and 15${\cdot}$8\% in the elderly. On
extreme cold days ($T_\mathrm{min}$ $<$P10), admissions increased
by 35\% in children $<$5~years and 33\% in adults $\geq$65~years
compared with warm days ($T_\mathrm{min}$ $>$P75), versus 14\% in
the 5--64 age group. Lagged cross-correlations identified distinct
temporal structures: the $\geq$65 group exhibited the strongest
delayed association with $T_\mathrm{min}$ (peak $r$=$-$0${\cdot}$36
at lag~8~days), matching cold-induced exacerbations of chronic
obstructive pulmonary disease, while children $<$5~years peaked at
lag~9~days ($r$=$-$0${\cdot}$25), compatible with the incubation
period of viral lower respiratory infections. The dominant diagnoses
confirmed these pathways: acute lower respiratory infections
(J20--J22) were overwhelmingly paediatric (90${\cdot}$4\% aged
$<$5), whereas pneumonia (J09--J18) and COPD/asthma (J40--J47) were
concentrated in the $\geq$65 group (48${\cdot}$4\% and
46${\cdot}$8\%, respectively).

\paragraph{Age- and diagnosis-stratified DLNMs}
To move beyond descriptive associations, we fitted separate quasi-Poisson
DLNMs (same specification as the primary model) for three age groups and
two diagnostic categories (appendix table~15). Pneumonia (J09--J18)
exhibited the strongest cold effect: cumulative RR at P1 of
2${\cdot}$08 [1${\cdot}$43--3${\cdot}$01], roughly double the risk.
Among age groups, adults 5--64~years showed the largest effect
(RR=1${\cdot}$75 [1${\cdot}$16--2${\cdot}$63] at P1;
1${\cdot}$34 [1${\cdot}$04--1${\cdot}$72] at P10), followed by
adults $\geq$65~years (RR=1${\cdot}$63 [1${\cdot}$10--2${\cdot}$40]
at P1). Children $<$5~years showed a non-significant effect
(RR=1${\cdot}$20 [0${\cdot}$69--2${\cdot}$10]), likely reflecting
limited statistical power from low daily counts (mean
6${\cdot}$9/day). COPD/asthma (J40--J47) was borderline significant
(RR=1${\cdot}$72 [0${\cdot}$99--2${\cdot}$98]) with a lower MMT of
15${\cdot}$6\,°C, suggesting chronic vulnerability to moderate cold.
Heat effects remained non-significant across all strata. These
stratified models confirm that the aggregate cold effect is driven by
biologically plausible pathways rather than confounding, and identify
pneumonia as the diagnosis most amenable to cold-triggered
interventions. Sex-stratified DLNMs revealed that women were
more vulnerable across the cold spectrum (RR=1${\cdot}$69
[1${\cdot}$20--2${\cdot}$40] at P1; 1${\cdot}$45
[1${\cdot}$17--1${\cdot}$79] at P10; MMT=18${\cdot}$1\,°C), while
men showed significant effects only at P1 (RR=1${\cdot}$56
[1${\cdot}$15--2${\cdot}$12]; MMT=11${\cdot}$6\,°C). The
sex difference at P10 was statistically significant
($z$=2${\cdot}$85, $p$=0${\cdot}$004; Altman--Bland
test\cite{Altman2003}), whereas at P1 both sexes were equally
affected ($p$=0${\cdot}$73), suggesting that sex-differential
vulnerability is most pronounced at moderate rather than extreme cold
(appendix table~16).

\subsection{Clustering: structural-exploratory evidence}

K-means clustering ($K$=3, silhouette=0${\cdot}$357) identified three
regimes: `Clean Cold' ($n$=248), `Polluted Heat' ($n$=12), and `Clean Heat'
($n$=298; appendix tables~2--3, appendix figures~1--4).\cite{Rousseeuw1987} The
`Clean Cold' cluster exhibited delayed correlations with
hospitalisations peaking at lags 3--10~days --- subsequently confirmed
by the DLNM (§\ref{sec:dlnm_results}). A $K$=4 validation showed
stronger between-cluster differences (Kruskal--Wallis $H$=16${\cdot}$69,
$p$$<$0${\cdot}$001; appendix table~3), with the `Polluted Heat' cluster
($\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$=51${\cdot}$5~µg/m$^3$) carrying the highest
hospitalisation burden (38${\cdot}$6/day vs.\ 29${\cdot}$4 in `Clean Heat').

PCA corroborated this: PC1 (44${\cdot}$5\% of variance) linked cold
temperatures, elevated $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$, and higher admissions in a
single dimension (appendix table~4). Extended lag analysis confirmed peak
$T_\mathrm{min}$--hospitalisation correlation at lag~9
($r$=$-$0${\cdot}$320; appendix table~5), with $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ showing
weaker but significant delayed associations (peak $r$=$+$0${\cdot}$172
at lag~13).

\subsection{Distributed lag non-linear models}
\label{sec:dlnm_results}

\paragraph{Temperature exposure--response}
The quasi-Poisson DLNM ($N$=1068, $\hat{\phi}$=1${\cdot}$32)
identified an MMT of 17${\cdot}$8\,°C, aligned with the local 75th
percentile (Figure~\ref{fig:dlnm_temp_overall}). Below this threshold,
risk rose steeply: at P1 (5${\cdot}$2\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$42
[1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84]; at P5 (8${\cdot}$5\,°C),
RR=1${\cdot}$22 [1${\cdot}$02--1${\cdot}$44]; at P10
(9${\cdot}$8\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$17 [1${\cdot}$00--1${\cdot}$37]
(Table~\ref{tab:dlnm_temp_rr}). Heat effects were non-significant
(P90 RR=1${\cdot}$06 [0${\cdot}$98--1${\cdot}$14]). The lag
structure peaked at 3--10~days (Figure~\ref{fig:dlnm_temp_lag}a),
confirming the clustering pattern.

\paragraph{Sensitivity and diagnostics}
The cold effect was robust to all pre-specified sensitivity analyses
(Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}). Cumulative RR at P10 ranged from
1${\cdot}$05 (lag=14~days, capturing only the early phase of the
delayed response) to 1${\cdot}$23 (lag=28~days). Excluding
2022 --- the year with the greatest residual COVID-19 influence ---
strengthened the cold effect to RR=1${\cdot}$37
[1${\cdot}$11--1${\cdot}$70], suggesting that pandemic-related
disruptions attenuated the temperature--morbidity signal in the full
period. Excluding 2023 yielded consistent results (RR=1${\cdot}$27
[1${\cdot}$05--1${\cdot}$54]), confirming temporal stability across
non-pandemic years. ADF tests confirmed non-stationarity in raw hospitalisations
($p$=0${\cdot}$16) but stationarity in STL residuals
($p$=0${\cdot}$0003; appendix figure~7), validating the detrending approach.
Model diagnostics showed no residual autocorrelation
(Durbin--Watson=2${\cdot}$00, Ljung--Box $p$=0${\cdot}$14;
appendix table~7).

\paragraph{Exploratory $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ analysis}
An exploratory DLNM for EPA-corrected $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ ($N$=697,
lag 0--14~days) showed no significant cumulative associations: at the
90th percentile (10${\cdot}$2~µg/m$^3$), RR=0${\cdot}$91
[0${\cdot}$78--1${\cdot}$07] (appendix figure~6). A joint temperature--$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$
model ($N$=683) yielded RERI=0${\cdot}$013 [$-$0${\cdot}$006,
0${\cdot}$033], indicating no supra-additive interaction on the
additive scale (appendix table~6). The A-B-C confounding diagnostic
(appendix table~9) confirmed that $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ does not confound the
primary temperature association ($\Delta$RR$<$0${\cdot}$001 between
Models~B and~C). To strictly avoid selective reporting, we emphasise this null
inferential finding. These null results should be interpreted in the
context of low ambient concentrations (median~4${\cdot}$1~µg/m$^3$)
and 36${\cdot}$4\% non-MCAR sensor data missingness, which severely
limited statistical power (appendix pp~2--5). Consequently,
the secondary $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ signals detected by PCA and ML models
must be interpreted strictly as hypothesis-generating.

\subsection{Machine learning: predictive convergence evidence}

\paragraph{Model performance and SHAP feature importance}
XGBoost achieved the best performance at the 1-day horizon
(MAPE=18${\cdot}$0\%, RMSE=8${\cdot}$10; appendix table~8, appendix figure~10),
maintaining MAPE below 21\% across all horizons (0--7~days). SHAP
analysis (appendix figure~8) ranked environmental features as: temperature
(mean $|\text{SHAP}|$=0${\cdot}$23), $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$
(0${\cdot}$16), and humidity (0${\cdot}$05). Temperature was the
dominant environmental predictor; $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ ranked second at
70\% of temperature's importance, with low temperatures and high
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ independently increasing predicted hospitalisations
--- in agreement with DLNM findings despite fundamentally different
modelling assumptions.

\paragraph{Exploratory scenario simulations}
Applying ML models to CMIP6 SSP5-8${\cdot}$5 projections for 2050
(appendix figures~11--13), Monte Carlo simulations of compound extreme events
projected weekly hospitalisation increases of up to 17\% during cold
waves and 55\% during wildfire $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ episodes, concentrated
in autumn and early winter. These exploratory scenarios, while
inherently uncertain, demonstrate the operational potential of ML
models for climate-adaptive early-warning systems.

Together, clustering (exploratory), DLNM (inferential), and ML/SHAP
(predictive) converge: cold dominates the temperature--hospitalisation
association, with $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ as a consistent secondary signal
across data-driven methods.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Principal findings}

Cold extremes --- not heat --- exhibited the strongest association
with respiratory hospitalisations in this subtropical city
(RR=1${\cdot}$42 [1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84] at P1; heat
non-significant), with a delayed effect peaking at 3--10~days that
provides a clinically useful intervention window. $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$
was consistently identified as a secondary co-factor across
exploratory and predictive approaches.

\subsection{Cold-dominant burden: rethinking the heat-centric approach}

Our findings complicate the dominant framing that equates rising
temperatures with rising health
risk.\cite{Bell2024,McMichael2006} Cold accounted for only
3${\cdot}$7\% of extreme-temperature days yet was the sole exposure
with significant excess morbidity --- a low-frequency, high-impact
pattern current surveillance systems cannot detect. This is
consistent with Gasparrini et~al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} and the
global temperature--mortality burden estimated by Zhao
et~al.,\cite{Zhao2021} but extends the evidence from mortality to
\emph{morbidity} and applies a triangulation design not previously
used in temperature--health studies. Our MMT of 17${\cdot}$8\,°C aligns with subtropical MMTs
in multi-country analyses.\cite{Gasparrini2015} A heat-exclusive early-warning system would miss the largest
temperature-attributable morbidity signal, echoing recent
temperature--hospitalisation projections for Chile that also
highlighted cold-season vulnerability in subtropical
latitudes.\cite{PalmeiroSilva2025}

\subsection{Pathophysiological plausibility and lag structure}

The 3--10-day lag is biologically coherent: cold suppresses
mucociliary clearance and epithelial integrity (hours), impairs
macrophage function increasing infection susceptibility (1--3~days),
followed by clinical illness and hospitalisation
(3--10~days).\cite{Linares2025,Ebi2021} This delayed sequence
explains why cold-attributable morbidity is underdetected by
surveillance systems operating on 0--48-hour event windows.

In subtropical Brazilian cities, cold-driven indoor crowding is
amplified by housing insulation deficits absent in temperate
regions,\cite{Cheng2024} disproportionately affecting marginalised
communities and creating a `hidden vulnerability'
scenario.\cite{Gasparrini2015,Cohen2017,Zscheischler2020,Gouveia2003} Our
age-stratified analyses support this gradient empirically:
in-hospital mortality was 53-fold higher in adults $\geq$65~years
(15${\cdot}$8\%) than in children $<$5~years (0${\cdot}$3\%).

\subsection{Robustness of findings}

The cold effect was stable across all sensitivity analyses: lag
windows (14--28~days), seasonal df (6--8/year), and time periods
(Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}). The effect remained significant when
excluding each study year individually (2022: RR=1${\cdot}$37; 2023:
RR=1${\cdot}$27), confirming temporal stability. A likelihood ratio
test comparing the non-linear spline model against a linear
exposure--response found no significant departure from linearity
($F$=1${\cdot}$36, $p$=0${\cdot}$16), indicating a predominantly
monotonic cold-risk relationship below the MMT --- consistent with
subtropical patterns in Gasparrini
et~al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} --- and confirming that the spline
specification is conservative rather than artificially inflating the
effect. The E-value\cite{VanderWeele2017} for the primary finding
(RR=1${\cdot}$42 at P1) was 2${\cdot}$19, meaning an unmeasured
confounder would need to be associated with both cold exposure and
respiratory hospitalisation by a factor of $\geq$2${\cdot}$2 to
explain the observed effect, which exceeds the magnitude of most
plausible unmeasured confounders in this context. The three analytical approaches --- differing in assumptions,
optimisation criteria, and outputs --- converged on the same
conclusion: cold dominates the environmental--hospitalisation
association, with $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ as a secondary co-factor detectable
by data-driven methods (PCA/clustering, SHAP) though not by the DLNM
alone, likely reflecting insufficient power rather than absence of
effect.

\subsection{Air pollution as a structural co-factor}

Despite low ambient concentrations (median~4${\cdot}$12~µg/m$^3$),
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ was consistently identified as a secondary co-factor
by PCA, clustering, and SHAP --- a finding of practical importance given that these signals
emerge below WHO guideline levels.\cite{Cheng2024} The DLNM
did not reach significance, likely reflecting limited power from low
concentrations and 36${\cdot}$4\% data missingness. In settings with
higher pollution or increased wildfire smoke, this pathway may become
inferentially detectable.

\subsection{Policy implications}

Brazil's VIGIAR-SUS currently lacks a cold-specific trigger for
respiratory alerts.\cite{Bell2024} The 3--10-day delayed peak
provides a concrete operational timeframe: Days~0--2, Primary Health
Care (ESF) teams initiate tele-monitoring of vulnerable patients;
Days~3--5, Emergency Care Units (UPAs) reinforce oxygen and
bronchodilator stocks. Because women exhibited significantly higher
cold vulnerability than men (RR=1${\cdot}$69 vs.\ 1${\cdot}$56 at P1;
appendix table~16), sex-targeted interventions may further improve
efficiency. As a single-city study, multi-city replication using the
extended
meta-analytic
framework\cite{Sera2019,Gasparrini2015} is needed before national
policy.\cite{Requia2024}

\subsection{Limitations}

This study has limitations. First, as an ecological
study, we cannot infer individual-level causation, though this design
is appropriate for informing early-warning systems. Second, the
3-year period encompasses only four cold-spell events; however, the
DLNM uses the full continuous temperature distribution ($N$=1068),
and three independent methods consistently reproduced the cold effect.

Third, confounding by respiratory viral circulation (influenza, RSV)
is a concern given that the 3--10~day lag overlaps with viral
incubation periods. To address this, we obtained weekly SRAG
(Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome) surveillance data from
SIVEP-Gripe for Curitiba (2022--2024; $n$=28\,972 notifications)
and conducted a post-hoc sensitivity analysis (appendix table~14).
Overall viral positivity was \emph{lower} during cold weeks than
warm weeks (0${\cdot}$32 vs.\ 0${\cdot}$45), driven by COVID-19's
warm-season pattern. In a multivariate weekly model, controlling for
influenza, RSV, and COVID-19 counts attenuated the
$T_\mathrm{min}$--hospitalisation coefficient by only
15${\cdot}$8\%, and the partial correlation remained highly
significant ($r$=$-$0${\cdot}$58, $p$$<$0${\cdot}$001). Adding
$T_\mathrm{min}$ to a viral-only model increased $R^2$ from
0${\cdot}$36 to 0${\cdot}$60, confirming a substantial independent
cold effect beyond viral mediation. Nevertheless, fine-grained
weekly viral data cannot fully replace individual-level adjustment,
and the 3--10~day lag likely captures both direct physiological
cold stress and weather-facilitated viral transmission.

Fourth, our primary analysis used aggregate respiratory admissions
(ICD-10 J00--J99). However, age-stratified descriptive analyses
revealed distinct vulnerability profiles: children $<$5~years
(21${\cdot}$4\% of admissions) showed the highest relative increase
on cold days (35\%) with acute lower respiratory infections
(J20--J22) comprising 90${\cdot}$4\% of their diagnoses, while
adults $\geq$65~years (32${\cdot}$7\%) exhibited a 33\% cold-day
increase dominated by pneumonia (48${\cdot}$4\%) and COPD/asthma
(46${\cdot}$8\%). These age-specific lag structures (peak at lag~8
for elderly, lag~9 for children) support the biological plausibility
of the aggregate cold effect and suggest that future early-warning
implementations should tailor clinical responses by age group.
Although race and ethnicity data are partially collected in the
Brazilian DATASUS registry, high missingness rates precluded their
inclusion in this aggregate design. Age-, diagnosis-, and sex-stratified DLNMs confirmed the aggregate
finding with notable heterogeneity (appendix tables~15--16): women
showed significant cold effects across the full spectrum
(P1 through P25), while men were significant only at P1. Similarly, future studies should stratify race and
ethnicity to identify disproportionate burdens among
socio-demographically minoritised populations.

Fifth, single-station temperature data may miss microclimatic
variation, though cold extremes are driven by synoptic-scale frontal
systems affecting the region uniformly. Sixth, $\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$
from a single low-cost sensor had 36${\cdot}$4\% missing values (not
MCAR; appendix table~10), though empirical mapping confirmed central sensors
capture metropolitan temporal variance ($r$$>$0${\cdot}$76),
\cite{daCosta2024} and the A-B-C diagnostic (appendix table~9) confirmed
$\text{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}$ does not confound the temperature association.
Seventh, 2050 simulations hold demographics constant and serve as
illustrative scenarios rather than forecasts. Finally, as a
single-city study, multi-site replication is needed.

\subsection{Conclusion}

In Curitiba, cold extremes --- not heat --- drive the largest
temperature-related respiratory hospitalisation burden, with a
delayed 3--10-day effect confirmed by three independent analytical
approaches. These findings identify a remediable gap in subtropical
South American climate--health surveillance: the absence of
cold-spell triggers in early-warning systems. Integrating a
temperature-threshold alert at the identified MMT
(17${\cdot}$8\,°C) into existing frameworks warrants evaluation as a
strategy to reduce preventable respiratory morbidity in the region.
