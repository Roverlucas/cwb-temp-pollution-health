\section*{Supplementary Methods}

\subsection*{S.1\quad Exploratory PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ DLNM and confounding assessment}

An exploratory DLNM for EPA-corrected PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ ($N$=697,
lag 0--14~days, exposure df=4, lag df=4) showed no significant
cumulative associations at any percentile (eFigure~6): P90
(10${\cdot}$2~┬Ág/m$^3$) RR=0${\cdot}$91 [0${\cdot}$78--1${\cdot}$07].
The low ambient concentrations (median 4${\cdot}$1~┬Ág/m$^3$, well
below the WHO 24-hour guideline) and 36${\cdot}$4\% missing data
likely limited statistical power. A joint model ($N$=683) yielded
RERI=0${\cdot}$013 [$-$0${\cdot}$006, 0${\cdot}$033] on the additive
scale, indicating no evidence of supra-additive interaction between
temperature and PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ (eTable~6).

\paragraph{Confounding diagnostic: A-B-C comparison (eTable~9)}
To distinguish PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ confounding from sample restriction
bias, we compared four models at P10 of $T_\mathrm{min}$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Model~A:} Unadjusted temperature DLNM on the full
    sample ($N$=1068, RR=1${\cdot}$17).
  \item \textbf{Model~B:} Unadjusted temperature DLNM restricted to
    PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$-complete days ($N$=683, RR=0${\cdot}$82) ---
    \emph{without} adjusting for PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$.
  \item \textbf{Model~C:} Temperature DLNM \emph{with} PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$
    as linear covariate ($N$=683, RR=0${\cdot}$82).
  \item \textbf{Model~D:} Temperature DLNM with relative humidity as
    linear covariate ($N$=851, RR=1${\cdot}$18).
\end{itemize}
Models~B and~C are virtually identical ($\Delta$RR$<$0${\cdot}$001),
proving that PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ does not confound the
temperature--hospitalisation association. The entire attenuation from
Model~A to Model~B (30\% reduction) is due to restricting the sample
to PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$-available days, which systematically
underrepresent cold temperatures (eTable~10). Model~D confirms that
humidity does not confound the primary estimates.

Atmospheric pressure was not available in the merged daily dataset and
could not be included; however, pressure's effect on respiratory
admissions is predominantly mediated through
temperature,\cite{Linares2025} and its omission is unlikely to bias
the primary temperature estimates substantially.

\subsection*{Missing data sensitivity}

\paragraph{PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ missingness mechanism (eTables~10--11)}
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ data were available for 697 of 1096~study days
(63${\cdot}$6\%). Missingness was driven by PurpleAir sensor downtime
(hardware resets, network outages) and inter-channel quality control
exclusions. Days with missing PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ had significantly lower
temperatures ($-$1${\cdot}$4\,┬░C, $p<$0${\cdot}$001) and higher
hospitalisations ($+$3${\cdot}$4/day, $p<$0${\cdot}$001; eTable~10),
confirming that missingness is not MCAR.

To characterise the mechanism further, we fitted a logistic model:
$\mathrm{P}(\text{PM}_{2.5}\text{ observed}) \sim
\mathrm{ns}(T_\mathrm{min}, 4\,\text{df}) + \mathrm{month} +
\mathrm{DOW}$ (eTable~11). The model achieved pseudo-$R^2$=0${\cdot}$26
and AUC=0${\cdot}$80, confirming that season and temperature
strongly predict missingness. Conditional on these covariates, no
additional pattern is apparent, consistent with a missing-at-random
(MAR) mechanism driven by seasonal sensor downtime rather than by
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ levels themselves.

Consequently, complete-case analyses restricted to
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$-available days underrepresent cold temperatures and
produce attenuated temperature effect estimates. The A-B-C diagnostic
(eTable~9) demonstrates that this attenuation is entirely attributable
to sample restriction, not PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ confounding. The primary
temperature DLNM, which uses the full dataset ($N$=1068) without
conditioning on PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ availability, is not affected.

\paragraph{Imputation sensitivity}
Sensitivity analyses with mean imputation and $k$-nearest-neighbour
imputation ($k$=5) produced qualitatively similar clustering solutions
to the primary complete-case analysis (silhouette scores within
0${\cdot}$02 of the main analysis). PCA loadings and explained
variance were robust to imputation method.

\subsection*{S.2\quad Clustering and PCA}

K-means clustering was applied to six standardised variables
($T_\mathrm{max}$, $T_\mathrm{med}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$,
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$, relative humidity, atmospheric pressure) on
$N$=558 complete-case days. Optimal $K$=3 was selected by silhouette
score (0${\cdot}$357). Three clusters emerged: Clean Cold ($n$=248),
Polluted Heat ($n$=12), and Clean Heat ($n$=298). Kruskal--Wallis
test for between-cluster differences in daily hospitalisations
yielded $p$=0${\cdot}$039 (eTables~2--3). PCA on PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$,
$T_\mathrm{min}$, and hospitalisations identified PC1 (44${\cdot}$5\%
variance) associating cold, polluted days with higher admissions
(eTable~4). Extended lag correlations (eTable~5) revealed peak
$T_\mathrm{min}$--hospitalisation correlation at lag~9
($r$=$-$0${\cdot}$320, $p<$0${\cdot}$001).

\subsection*{S.3\quad Machine learning forecasting details}

\paragraph{Extreme Learning Machine (ELM)}
A single-hidden-layer feedforward network with random input weights
and regularised pseudoinverse output weights. Hyperparameter: number
of hidden neurons (scanned over 1--200).

\paragraph{Multilayer perceptron (MLP)}
A PyTorch-based feedforward network with one hidden layer (tanh
activation), trained with Adam optimiser (learning rate 0${\cdot}$01),
mini-batch size 20, early stopping (patience 10), and learning-rate
scheduling.

\paragraph{XGBoost}
A gradient-boosted tree ensemble (max depth 5, learning rate
0${\cdot}$1, \texttt{reg:squarederror} objective). The number of
estimators was tuned via validation-set MSE.

\paragraph{Training protocol}
Data were split temporally: 70\% training, 15\% validation, 15\%
test. Features were standardised (zero mean, unit variance). Model
selection used MAPE on the validation set; final evaluation used the
test set after retraining on training+validation data. SHAP (SHapley
Additive exPlanations) values were computed for the XGBoost model to
quantify feature importance.

\paragraph{Climate projections}
Future temperature scenarios were derived from the CMIP6 SSP5-8${\cdot}$5
pathway (EC-Earth3-Veg-LR model) for Curitiba's grid cell, with daily
$T_\mathrm{max}$, $T_\mathrm{med}$, and specific humidity extracted
for 2030, 2050, and 2099. Future PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ was projected using
the most recent year's Transformer-imputed series as baseline.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo extreme-event simulations}
Heat waves were simulated by injecting random temperature perturbations
(+2--5\,┬░C for 3--7~days) during autumn; cold waves by analogous
decrements during spring/autumn. Wildfire PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ spikes were
simulated by adding 70--100~┬Ág/m$^3$ pulses (7--14~days) during
summer/spring.
