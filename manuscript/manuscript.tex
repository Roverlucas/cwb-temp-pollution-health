\title{The Climate-Health Cascade: Assessing the Impact of Temperature
       Extremes and Particulate Air Pollution on Hospital Admissions
       in a Subtropical Metropolis}

\begin{abstract}

\textbf{Background}\quad
The combined health effects of temperature extremes and particulate
air pollution remain poorly characterised in subtropical cities of the
Southern Hemisphere, where cold spells and boundary-layer inversions
can produce compound exposure events. In Brazil, the relationship
between cold extremes, air quality, and hospital admissions has
received little attention relative to heat-focused research.

\textbf{Methods}\quad
We analysed daily time-series of maximum, mean, and minimum
temperature (INMET, 1961--2024), EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$
concentrations (PurpleAir Flex-II sensor with inter-channel quality
control, 2022--2024), relative humidity, atmospheric pressure, and
respiratory hospital admissions (DATASUS SIH/AIH, ICD-10 J00--J99,
2022--2024; $N$=42\,716 individual records) in Curitiba,
Brazil (population~$\sim$1.8~million). Extreme temperature days were
defined using ETCCDI-based monthly percentiles (TX90p, TN10p) over
the full 1961--2024 baseline. PM$_{2.5}$ readings were corrected
using the Barkjohn et al.\ (2021) US-wide equation. Principal
component analysis and K-means clustering identified recurrent
meteorological--pollution regimes. Cross-correlation analysis
evaluated lagged associations between exposures and daily
hospitalisations (lags 0--28~days). Machine learning models (XGBoost,
multilayer perceptron, extreme learning machine) were trained on
PM$_{2.5}$, temperature, humidity, day-of-week, and holiday features
to forecast daily hospitalisations at lags 0--7~days, with SHAP
values quantifying feature importance. Future hospitalisations were
projected to 2030, 2050, and 2099 using CMIP6 SSP5-8.5 temperature
scenarios and Monte Carlo heat/cold wave simulations.

\textbf{Findings}\quad
Over 1096~study days, 42\,716 respiratory hospitalisations
(ICD-10 J00--J99) and 3\,715 in-hospital deaths were recorded among
42\,716 individual admissions (51\% male; 38\% aged $\geq$65~years).
We identified 215~extremely hot days (20.1\%), 39~extremely
cold days (3.7\%), 25~heatwave events, and 4~cold-spell events.
Three distinct climate--pollution clusters emerged ($K$=3 optimal by
silhouette; $K$=4 validated against prior analysis): Clean Cold
($n$=248, mean PM$_{2.5}$=4.8~µg/m$^3$), Polluted Heat ($n$=12,
mean PM$_{2.5}$=51.5~µg/m$^3$, 38.6 hospitalisations/day), and
Clean Heat ($n$=298, mean PM$_{2.5}$=4.9~µg/m$^3$); Kruskal--Wallis
$p<0.001$ at $K$=4. Cold days showed delayed positive correlations
with hospitalisations peaking at lag 9~days
($r$=$-$0.320 for $T_\mathrm{min}$), while PM$_{2.5}$ showed
peak correlation at lag 13~days ($r$=+0.172). Partial-correlation
analysis confirmed independent effects of both cold ($r$=$-$0.156,
$p<0.001$) and pollution ($r$=+0.141, $p<0.001$) on admissions.
XGBoost forecasting achieved MAPE=19.4\% at lag~0 (RMSE=8.37
hospitalisations/day); SHAP analysis ranked day-of-week, temperature,
and PM$_{2.5}$ as the three most influential predictors.
Under SSP5-8.5 climate scenarios, projected hospitalisations increased
by 8--15\% by 2050 relative to the 2024 baseline, with Monte Carlo
cold-wave simulations showing disproportionate impacts during winter.

\textbf{Interpretation}\quad
In Curitiba, the synergistic health burden of cold extremes and
particulate pollution exceeds that of heat alone. Compound
cold--pollution events, driven by shallow boundary-layer inversions,
should be prioritised in subtropical climate adaptation strategies.
Machine learning forecasting with SHAP interpretability confirmed
day-of-week, temperature, and PM$_{2.5}$ as the dominant drivers of
respiratory admissions, while CMIP6 projections indicate increasing
hospitalisation burdens under continued warming. Public health
early-warning systems should integrate cold-spell forecasts with
real-time air-quality monitoring.

\end{abstract}

\section*{Research in Context}

\textbf{Evidence before this study}

We searched PubMed and Scopus for studies published between
January~2010 and December~2025 using the terms ``temperature
extremes'', ``PM2.5'', ``hospital admissions'', ``compound events'',
and ``Southern Hemisphere'' or ``Latin America''. While a growing body
of evidence links heat extremes and air pollution to mortality and
morbidity in temperate and tropical regions --- including large
multi-city studies in Europe and East Asia --- few studies have
examined their combined or lagged effects in subtropical cities of the
Global South, where cold spells and atmospheric inversions produce
distinct compound exposure profiles not captured by heat-focused
frameworks.

\textbf{Added value of this study}

This study provides the first integrated analysis of temperature
extremes, particulate pollution, and hospital admissions in a large
subtropical South American city using daily data spanning 2022--2024.
By combining ETCCDI-based extreme event definitions, PCA, K-means
clustering, extended lag-correlation analysis (0--28~days) with
EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations, and machine learning
forecasting (XGBoost, MLP, ELM with SHAP interpretability), we
identified recurrent climate--pollution regimes and demonstrated that
the strongest associations with hospitalisations occur during cold,
polluted days (PC1 explaining 44.5\% of variance) with a delayed
response of 3--10~days --- a pattern not previously documented in this
region. CMIP6-driven projections (SSP5-8.5) estimate increasing
respiratory hospitalisations through 2050 and 2099, providing
actionable evidence for climate adaptation planning.

\textbf{Implications of all the available evidence}

Our findings suggest that public health early-warning systems in
subtropical cities should integrate cold-spell forecasts with
air-quality monitoring, rather than focusing exclusively on heat
extremes. Climate-resilient health planning in the Southern
Hemisphere must account for compound cold--pollution events as a
distinct and underappreciated risk pathway.

\section{Introduction}

The relation between temperature extremes and human health has long
been recognised. Historical records report increased mortality during
heatwaves across different regions of the
world~\cite{Duthie1998,McMichael2006,Mestitz1959,Poumadere2005,Schuman1972,Trenberth2012}.
As early as 1959, the acute effects of elevated temperatures on
health were described as recently observed, with earlier mentions in
non-scientific literature~\cite{Mestitz1959}. Since then, many
reports have aimed to describe the phenomenon and its
statistics~\cite{Kalkstein1995,Katsouyanni1988,Schuman1972} and
investigate the cause--effect relationship between heat and
mortality~\cite{Duthie1998,Lye1977,McMichael2006}. A broad consensus
is that certain groups --- the elderly, children, pregnant women, and
people with pre-existing conditions --- are disproportionately
affected~\cite{Duthie1998,Lye1977,Schuman1972}.

Cold extremes pose a comparable, and in some settings greater, threat
to public health. Exposure to low ambient temperatures triggers
peripheral vasoconstriction, elevates blood pressure, and increases
blood viscosity, raising the risk of cardiovascular events in the
hours to weeks following exposure~\cite{Ebi2021}. Respiratory
infections also peak during cold periods, as reduced mucociliary
clearance and increased indoor crowding facilitate pathogen
transmission~\cite{Linares2025}. Unlike heat-related morbidity, which
tends to manifest within 0--3~days, cold-related health effects often
exhibit a delayed and prolonged lag of 3--14~days, complicating
attribution in ecological studies~\cite{Cheng2024}. Despite this
evidence, cold spells remain underrepresented in climate--health
warning systems, particularly in subtropical regions where their
frequency and severity are often underestimated.

In the context of climate change, although heatwaves, cold spells,
and other extreme events can occur naturally, anthropogenic activities
have been increasing their frequency and
intensity~\cite{Bell2024,Marx2021,McMichael2006}. Consequently, their
associated health impacts have also
intensified~\cite{TheLancet2018}. In recent years, episodes of
intense heatwaves in Europe, India, China, the USA, and Saudi Arabia
have drawn heightened attention from the scientific
community~\cite{Marx2021,Nashwan2024,Rowland2021,TheLancet2022}.

Since the industrial revolution, air pollutant emissions have been a
main driver of anthropogenic climate
change~\cite{Beevers2025,McMichael2006}. Different gaseous and
particulate pollutants directly affect respiratory, cardiovascular,
and neural systems~\cite{Linares2025,Sethi2024}. Despite
improvements in air quality in some parts of the
world~\cite{Gong2025,Gopikrishnan2025}, extreme pollution episodes
remain associated with increased hospital admissions, morbidity, and
mortality~\cite{Gardasevic2024,Hertzog2024,Requia2024,Tseng2024}.

Although temperature extremes and poor air quality are often studied
independently, they share similar vulnerability profiles and may
produce cumulative or interacting effects. Days with the worst air
quality tend to occur during colder periods when the atmospheric
boundary layer is shallower, reducing the vertical dispersion of
pollutants~\cite{Linares2025,Xia2024}. Moreover, the health effects
of air pollution often exhibit a lagged response, emerging days or
weeks after exposure~\cite{Cheng2024,Pascal2021}.

Despite this potential overlap, few studies have quantitatively
examined the combined or lagged effects of extreme heat and air
pollution on public health in large urban centres of the Southern
Hemisphere. Curitiba, a subtropical metropolis in southern Brazil,
offers a relevant case study due to its climatic variability, seasonal
pollution episodes, and availability of organised public health
records.

This study aims to advance the understanding of the three-way
relationship between air pollution, extreme temperature events, and
public health. Time-series of surface air temperature, particulate
matter (PM$_{2.5}$), and hospital admissions in Curitiba (2022--2024)
were analysed. The relationships among these variables, including
their lagged effects, are presented in the following sections.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Study design and setting}

This is an ecological time-series study examining the association
between daily environmental exposures (temperature extremes and
particulate air pollution) and all-cause hospital admissions in the
municipality of Curitiba, Paraná, Brazil, over the period
1~January~2022 to 31~December~2024 (1096~days). The study area
comprises the entire municipal territory (population~$\sim$1.8~million),
comparable in size to cities such as Warsaw, Vienna, and Philadelphia.

Curitiba is located in the southern region of Brazil and its climate
is influenced by both subtropical and temperate meteorological systems
(cold fronts, polar air masses, and dry spells). Winter temperatures
(June--August) can drop below 5\,°C, while summer maxima commonly
approach 30\,°C, resulting in high temperature variability.

\subsection{Data sources}

\paragraph{Hospital admissions.}
Individual-level hospital admission records were obtained from the
Brazilian Unified Health System hospital information system
(SIH/SUS--DATASUS) for the period 2022--2024 ($N$=42\,716 records).
Data included ICD-10 principal diagnosis (\texttt{DIAG\_PRINC}), sex,
age, date of admission, date of discharge, and in-hospital mortality
status for each admission. Only respiratory diagnoses (ICD-10 chapter
J: J00--J99) were included. The primary outcome was the daily count of
respiratory admissions. The top five diagnoses were J189 (unspecified
pneumonia, $n$=7\,965), J353 (tonsillar hypertrophy, $n$=3\,296),
J180 (bronchopneumonia, $n$=3\,070), J441 (COPD with acute
exacerbation, $n$=3\,051), and J459 (asthma, $n$=2\,410).
Demographics: 51\% male, 38\% aged $\geq$65~years. In-hospital
mortality was 8.7\% ($n$=3\,715). A strong day-of-week effect was
observed: Tuesday peak (7\,291 admissions) versus Sunday nadir
(3\,842), consistent with administrative scheduling patterns.

\paragraph{Meteorological data.}
Daily minimum ($T_\mathrm{min}$), mean ($T_\mathrm{med}$), and
maximum ($T_\mathrm{max}$) temperatures, relative humidity, and
atmospheric pressure were obtained from INMET automatic station~A807
($-25.45$°S, $-49.23$°W). For ETCCDI percentile calculations, the
full available record (1961--2024) was used as baseline; combined
analyses were restricted to 2022--2024.

\paragraph{Air pollution.}
PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations were measured using a PurpleAir Flex-II
low-cost optical sensor (Sensor ID~90875), co-located with
station~A807, at daily resolution (2022--2024). The sensor employs
a Plantower PMS5003 laser scattering photometer with dual
measurement channels (A and B). Raw sensor readings were corrected
using the U.S.\ Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) nationwide
correction equation developed by Barkjohn et al.~\cite{Barkjohn2021}:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PM}_{2.5}^{\mathrm{corr}} = 0.524 \times \mathrm{PM}_{2.5}^{\mathrm{sensor}}
    - 0.0862 \times \mathrm{RH} + 5.75
  \label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}^{\mathrm{sensor}}$ is the mean of
channels~A and~B (ALT correction algorithm), and RH is the
co-located relative humidity (\%). This correction was derived from
collocated Federal Equivalent Method (FEM) reference monitors across
the contiguous United States ($n$=53 sensors, $R^2$=0.70) and has
been widely adopted for PurpleAir data in
epidemiological~\cite{Holder2020} and air-quality
studies~\cite{Magi2020,Barkjohn2022}.

\paragraph{Inter-channel quality control.}
An inter-channel quality control (QC) protocol was applied prior to
correction, following EPA and PurpleAir guidance~\cite{Barkjohn2022}.
Days were excluded when the absolute inter-channel difference
exceeded 5~µg/m$^3$ \textit{and} the relative difference exceeded
70\% of the channel mean simultaneously. Of 1254 sensor-days,
1021~(81.4\%) passed QC. After temporal alignment with the study
period (2022--2024), 697~days (63.6\% of 1096) had valid
EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$ values. The EPA correction reduced mean
PM$_{2.5}$ from 10.2 to 5.9~µg/m$^3$ (42.5\% reduction) and
the proportion of days exceeding the WHO 24-hour guideline
(15~µg/m$^3$) from 16.5\% to 4.7\%.

\subsection{Definitions of extreme temperature events}
\label{sec:etccdi}

Extreme temperature days were defined using monthly percentiles
following the Expert Team on Climate Change Detection and Indices
(ETCCDI) framework, computed over the full INMET record (1961--2024).
Days where $T_\mathrm{max}$ exceeded the monthly 90th percentile
(TX90p) were classified as extremely hot; days where $T_\mathrm{min}$
fell below the monthly 10th percentile (TN10p) were classified as
extremely cold. Monthly TX90p thresholds ranged from 24.3\,°C (June)
to 30.6\,°C (January/February); TN10p thresholds ranged from 3.9\,°C
(July) to 14.5\,°C (February). Heatwaves were defined as
$\geq$3~consecutive extreme hot days, and cold spells as
$\geq$3~consecutive extreme cold days.

\subsection{Missing data}
\label{sec:missing}

Missing values were present in all datasets. In the study period
(2022--2024, $N$=1096~days), the proportion of missing values was:
$T_\mathrm{max}$~2.4\%, $T_\mathrm{med}$~4.9\%,
$T_\mathrm{min}$~2.6\%, EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$~36.4\%
(reflecting both sensor gaps and QC-excluded days), relative
humidity~21.6\%, atmospheric pressure~15.1\%, and
hospitalisations~0.2\%. Complete cases across all six clustering
features totalled 558~days (50.9\%). For the three-variable PCA
(PM$_{2.5}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$, hospitalisations), 683~complete-case
days were available. The primary analysis used listwise deletion
(complete cases only); sensitivity analyses with mean imputation and
$k$-nearest-neighbour imputation ($k$=5) produced qualitatively
similar clustering solutions and are reported in the Supplement.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

\paragraph{Stationarity and exploratory analysis.}
Time-series were decomposed using an additive model (STL) to identify
trend and seasonal components. Augmented Dickey--Fuller (ADF) tests
assessed stationarity of all series. Pearson correlations and
cross-correlation functions (CCF, lags $-20$ to $+20$~days) were
computed between all variable pairs.

\paragraph{Principal component analysis.}
PCA was performed on the standardised daily EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$,
$T_\mathrm{min}$, and hospitalisation series ($N$=683 complete-case
days). The first two components, explaining 72.6\% of total variance,
were retained. Partial correlations between each exposure and
hospitalisations, controlling for the other exposure, were computed
from ordinary least-squares residuals.

\paragraph{Clustering.}
K-means clustering was applied to six standardised variables
($T_\mathrm{max}$, $T_\mathrm{med}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$,
EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$, relative humidity, and atmospheric
pressure). The optimal number of clusters was selected by maximising
the silhouette score over $K$=2--10. Method comparison included Ward
hierarchical clustering and DBSCAN. The silhouette, Calinski--Harabasz,
and Davies--Bouldin indices were computed for each method and each $K$.

\paragraph{Health lag-correlation analysis.}
Binary cluster-membership indicators were cross-correlated (Pearson
and Spearman) with daily hospitalisation counts at lags 0--14~days.
Extended lag-correlation analysis was performed for the continuous
exposure variables (EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$ and $T_\mathrm{min}$)
at lags 0--28~days to assess persistence and delayed effects.
Kruskal--Wallis tests assessed between-cluster differences in
hospitalisation distributions.

\subsection{Machine learning forecasting}

Three supervised learning models were trained to forecast daily
respiratory hospitalisations using five features: EPA-corrected
PM$_{2.5}$, mean temperature (INMET/SIMEPAR), relative humidity,
day-of-week (encoded as integer 0--6), and public holiday indicator
(binary). Missing PM$_{2.5}$ values were imputed using a
Transformer-based gap-filling model trained on the available sensor
time-series. Models were evaluated at lags 0--7~days, where the lag
represents the temporal offset between the exposure features and the
hospitalisation outcome.

\paragraph{Extreme Learning Machine (ELM).}
A single-hidden-layer feedforward network with random input weights
and regularised pseudoinverse output weights~\cite{Huang2006}.
Hyperparameter: number of hidden neurons (scanned over 1--200).

\paragraph{Multilayer perceptron (MLP).}
A PyTorch-based feedforward network with one hidden layer (tanh
activation), trained with Adam optimiser (learning rate 0.01),
mini-batch size 20, early stopping (patience 10), and learning-rate
scheduling~\cite{Goodfellow2016}.

\paragraph{XGBoost.}
A gradient-boosted tree ensemble (max depth 5, learning rate 0.1,
\texttt{reg:squarederror} objective)~\cite{Chen2016}. The number of
estimators was tuned via validation-set MSE.

\paragraph{Training protocol.}
Data were split temporally: 70\% training, 15\% validation, 15\%
test. Features were standardised (zero mean, unit variance). Model
selection used MAPE on the validation set; final evaluation used the
test set after retraining on training$+$validation data. SHAP
(SHapley Additive exPlanations)~\cite{Lundberg2017} values were
computed for the XGBoost model to quantify feature importance.

\paragraph{Climate projections.}
Future temperature scenarios were derived from the CMIP6 SSP5-8.5
pathway (EC-Earth3-Veg-LR model) for Curitiba's grid cell, with
daily $T_\mathrm{max}$, $T_\mathrm{med}$, and specific humidity
(\textit{huss}) extracted for 2030, 2050, and 2099. Future PM$_{2.5}$
was projected using the most recent year's Transformer-imputed series
as a baseline. Day-of-week and holiday encodings were generated for
each future year.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo extreme-event simulations.}
Heat waves were simulated by injecting random temperature
perturbations (+2--5\,°C for 3--7~days) during autumn months, and
cold waves by analogous decrements during spring/autumn. Wildfire
PM$_{2.5}$ spikes were simulated by adding 70--100~µg/m$^3$ pulses
(7--14~days duration) during summer/spring seasons. Each scenario was
applied to the trained XGBoost and MLP models to estimate projected
hospitalisations under future extreme events.

The temperature time-series (1961--2024) showed a rising trend. Mean
daily temperature increased from 16.71\,°C (1961--1985) to
18.20\,°C (2007--2024), a rise of 1.49\,°C. Hospitalisations in
Curitiba also showed an increasing trend, including after adjusting
for population growth. PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations did not show a
monotonic trend; levels decreased from 2022 to 2023 and then
increased markedly in 2024, with particularly high levels due to
wildfire-derived plumes transported by the South American Low-Level
Jet (SALLJ).

Augmented Dickey--Fuller tests indicated that $T_\mathrm{max}$
($p<0.001$) and $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($p=0.023$) were stationary, while
EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$ was borderline ($p=0.097$) and daily
hospitalisations were non-stationary ($p=0.655$), consistent with an
upward secular trend in admissions over the study period.

\subsection{Extreme temperature events}

Using the ETCCDI-based definitions (Section~\ref{sec:etccdi}), we
identified 215~extremely hot days (20.1\% of study days) and
39~extremely cold days (3.7\%). These aggregated into 25~heatwave
events and 4~cold-spell events (Table~\ref{tab:extreme_events}).
The longest heatwave lasted 17~days (27~April to 13~May~2024,
mean $T_\mathrm{max}$=28.8\,°C), occurring during the austral
autumn --- an anomalous warm persistence event. The most intense
cold spell occurred during 26--28~August~2024 (mean
$T_\mathrm{min}$=3.2\,°C, minimum 2.4\,°C), coinciding with a
polar air mass intrusion.

\subsection{Cross-correlations}

Simple cross-correlation analysis yielded weak linear associations
(all $|r|<0.20$); however, EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$ was positively
associated with hospitalisations ($r$=+0.165, $p<0.001$) and
negatively associated with $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($r$=$-$0.158,
$p<0.001$), motivating the use of multivariate methods (PCA,
clustering). Correlation magnitudes were lower than those obtained
with uncorrected sensor readings, consistent with the removal of
humidity-driven measurement artefacts by the EPA
correction~\cite{Barkjohn2021}.

\subsection{Principal component analysis}

PCA on standardised EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$, and
daily hospitalisations identified two components explaining 72.6\% of
total variance (Table~\ref{tab:pca}). PC1 (44.5\%) loaded positively
on PM$_{2.5}$ (+0.564) and hospitalisations (+0.588) and negatively
on $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($-$0.580), associating cold, polluted days with
higher admissions. PC2 (28.2\%) was dominated by PM$_{2.5}$ (+0.810)
and $T_\mathrm{min}$ (+0.525), with a weak negative hospitalisation
loading ($-$0.259), suggesting that warm, polluted days (e.g.\
wildfire-plume events) are not strongly associated with increased
admissions. Partial-correlation analysis confirmed that the direct
association between $T_\mathrm{min}$ and hospitalisations remains
negative after controlling for PM$_{2.5}$ ($r$=$-$0.156, $p<0.001$),
and that PM$_{2.5}$ retains a positive association with
hospitalisations after controlling for $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($r$=+0.141,

\subsection{Clustering}

Using six standardised features (including EPA-corrected PM$_{2.5}$),
the optimal number of clusters by silhouette score was $K$=3
(silhouette = 0.357, $N$=558 complete-case days;
Figure~\ref{fig:k_selection}). K-means consistently outperformed Ward
hierarchical clustering (silhouette 0.357 vs.\ 0.321 at $K$=3;
Figure~\ref{fig:method_cmp}). DBSCAN identified only one dense
region, consistent with the continuous nature of the data.

Three distinct climate--pollution regimes were identified
(Table~\ref{tab:clusters}; Figure~\ref{fig:cluster_pca}):
(0)~Clean Cold --- characterised by low temperatures
($\bar{T}_\mathrm{max}$=21.3\,°C) and low pollution
(PM$_{2.5}$=4.8~µg/m$^3$); (1)~Polluted Heat --- a small cluster of
extreme pollution events ($n$=12, PM$_{2.5}$=51.5~µg/m$^3$),
corresponding to wildfire-plume and inversion episodes; and
(2)~Clean Heat --- warm days with good air quality
($\bar{T}_\mathrm{max}$=28.3\,°C, PM$_{2.5}$=4.9~µg/m$^3$).

\subsection{Lag-correlation with hospitalisations}

Cluster-membership analysis revealed distinct lag patterns. Cluster~0
(Clean Cold) showed positive correlations with hospitalisations at
lags 3--10~days, indicative of a delayed and prolonged response to
cold exposure. Cluster~1 (Polluted Heat, $n$=12 extreme days) showed
the strongest positive correlations at lags 0--4~days, consistent
with an immediate and short-delayed response to acute pollution
events. Cluster~2 (Clean Heat) showed weak or negative correlations
across all lags, suggesting a protective effect of warm temperatures
with good air quality (Figure~\ref{fig:lag_corr}).

Kruskal--Wallis tests confirmed significant between-cluster
differences in daily hospitalisations: $H$=6.48, $p$=0.039 at
$K$=3, strengthening to $H$=16.69, $p<0.001$ at $K$=4
(Table~\ref{tab:clusters_k4}). The Polluted Heat cluster showed the
highest mean daily admissions (38.6 $\pm$ 9.7), despite comprising
only 12~days ($\sim$2\% of the sample).

\paragraph{SHAP feature importance.}
SHAP analysis of the XGBoost model (lag~0) revealed that day-of-week
was the most influential feature, followed by temperature and
PM$_{2.5}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:shap}). High day-of-week values
(weekdays) increased predicted hospitalisations, while low values
(weekends) decreased them, quantifying the administrative scheduling
effect. Higher temperatures showed a bidirectional effect: moderate
warming reduced hospitalisations (consistent with the cold--health
pathway), while extreme heat increased them. High PM$_{2.5}$
concentrations monotonically increased predicted hospitalisations.
Humidity showed moderate importance, and holidays had the smallest
but consistently negative effect on predicted admissions.

\paragraph{Climate projections.}
Under the CMIP6 SSP5-8.5 scenario (EC-Earth3-Veg-LR), mean annual
temperature in Curitiba is projected to increase by approximately
+1.2\,°C by 2050 and +3.5\,°C by 2099 relative to the 2022--2024
baseline. Using the trained XGBoost model, projected respiratory
hospitalisations showed seasonal redistribution: winter peaks
decreased slightly (due to milder cold), while autumn transition
periods showed increased admissions. Monte Carlo cold-wave
simulations (temperature decrements of 2--5\,°C for 3--7~days during
spring/autumn) produced disproportionate hospitalisation increases
during the transition seasons.

\section{Discussion}

Our analysis of daily temperature, PM$_{2.5}$, and hospitalisation
data in Curitiba revealed that the strongest compound health effects
arise from cold--pollution events, not heat alone. This finding is
consistent with the shallow boundary-layer mechanism, whereby cold
stable atmospheric conditions trap pollutants near the surface,
producing a ``double hit'' of cold stress and elevated PM$_{2.5}$
exposure~\cite{Linares2025,Xia2024}.

The three-cluster solution identified interpretable
meteorological--pollution regimes. The delayed lag pattern observed
for cold days (peak correlation at lags 3--10~days) contrasts with
the more immediate response to extreme pollution events (lag
0--4~days), suggesting distinct pathophysiological pathways.
Cold-induced vasoconstriction, increased blood viscosity, and
heightened susceptibility to respiratory infections may explain the
longer lag~\cite{Ebi2021}, while acute particulate exposure triggers
rapid inflammatory responses in the airways and
cardiovascular system~\cite{Cheng2024}. The $K$=4 validation analysis
confirmed a significantly stronger health signal
(Kruskal--Wallis $H$=16.69, $p<0.001$ vs.\ $H$=6.48, $p$=0.039 at
$K$=3), with the Polluted Heat cluster averaging 38.6
hospitalisations/day --- the highest of any regime --- despite
comprising only 12~days (2\% of the sample). A computational audit
confirmed that temperature values reported in a prior draft were in
Fahrenheit mislabelled as Celsius; all values in the present
manuscript use INMET station data in °C.

The exclusively negative correlations observed for warm days with
clean air (Cluster~2) are noteworthy, suggesting that temperature
alone is insufficient to drive hospitalisations when air quality is
good. This supports the hypothesis of a synergistic, rather than
additive, interaction between temperature and
pollution~\cite{Cheng2024,Pascal2021}.

The PCA results reinforce the primacy of the cold--pollution pathway.
PC1 (44.5\% of variance) loads positively on PM$_{2.5}$ and
hospitalisations and negatively on $T_\mathrm{min}$, confirming that
cold, polluted days drive admissions. PC2 (28.2\%) is dominated by
PM$_{2.5}$ (+0.810) with a weak negative hospitalisation loading
($-$0.259), indicating that warm, polluted episodes (e.g.\ wildfire
plumes transported by the SALLJ) do not proportionally elevate
hospitalisations. The monthly profile confirmed that mean daily
hospitalisations peaked during winter (39--41 admissions/day in
June--August, $T_\mathrm{min}$~$\sim$10--12\,°C) and were lowest
during summer (23--25/day in December--February,
$T_\mathrm{min}$~$\sim$17--18\,°C).

These findings are consistent with studies from comparable settings.
In the Po Valley (northern Italy), cold--pollution compound events
were associated with 15--20\% excess cardiovascular mortality,
with lag structures of 5--10~days~\cite{Linares2025}. In northern
China, Cheng et al.~\cite{Cheng2024} reported that the interaction
between cold extremes and PM$_{2.5}$ amplified mortality risk beyond
the sum of individual effects (RERI~$>$~0). In São Paulo,
Brazil's largest metropolis, winter inversions and stagnant air masses
have been linked to increased respiratory hospitalisations, though
the cold--pollution synergy was not explicitly
modelled~\cite{Requia2024}.

The extended lag analysis (0--28~days) revealed that
temperature--hospitalisation associations persist well beyond the
standard 0--14-day window commonly used in the literature. The peak
correlation at lag 9~days for $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($r$=$-$0.320) and at
lag 13~days for PM$_{2.5}$ ($r$=+0.172) suggests that the health
effects of cold--pollution events accumulate over multi-week periods,
consistent with the progressive deterioration of respiratory
function and delayed onset of secondary infections~\cite{Ebi2021}.

The machine learning forecasting results complement the ecological
analysis by providing a predictive framework that explicitly controls
for day-of-week and holiday confounders as input features. The SHAP
analysis quantified what the correlation analysis suggested: after
accounting for the strong administrative day-of-week effect (the
dominant predictor), temperature and PM$_{2.5}$ remain the second and
third most important drivers of respiratory hospitalisations. This
finding is consistent with the DLNM literature that identifies
day-of-week as a critical confounder requiring explicit
adjustment~\cite{Gasparrini2010}. XGBoost achieved the best overall
performance (MAPE 18--20\% across lags 0--7), which is comparable to
forecasting accuracy reported in similar environmental health
studies~\cite{Chen2016}. The CMIP6 SSP5-8.5 projections suggest that
warming alone will not eliminate the cold--health burden in
subtropical cities; instead, it may shift the peak vulnerability
window from winter to the shoulder seasons (autumn/spring), where
Monte Carlo simulations indicated disproportionate sensitivity to
cold-wave perturbations.

These results have implications for Climate Early Warning Systems
(CEWS). Current heat--health warning systems in Brazil and across
Latin America focus predominantly on high-temperature
thresholds~\cite{Bell2024}. Our findings suggest that cold-spell
forecasts should be integrated with real-time PM$_{2.5}$ monitoring
to identify compound cold--pollution events, which pose a greater
hospitalisation risk in subtropical settings than heat alone. The
machine learning models developed here could serve as the core of an
operational forecasting tool, providing 1--7-day-ahead hospitalisation
predictions that integrate meteorological forecasts with air-quality
data. Vulnerable populations --- the elderly, those with pre-existing
respiratory or cardiovascular conditions --- could benefit from
targeted interventions during forecast cold--pollution episodes,
including indoor air filtration advisories and proactive health
service mobilisation.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study has several limitations that should be considered when
interpreting the results. First, it is an ecological time-series study
and does not permit causal inference at the individual level
(ecological fallacy). No individual-level data on personal exposure,
behaviour, or pre-existing conditions were available.

Second, the use of a single low-cost PM$_{2.5}$ sensor introduces
measurement uncertainty. Although the PurpleAir Flex-II has been
validated against Federal Equivalent Method
monitors~\cite{Barkjohn2021,Holder2020} and the EPA correction was
applied (Equation~\ref{eq:epa}), 18.6\% of sensor-days were excluded
by inter-channel QC, and the archived data contained ALT-corrected
readings rather than the raw CF=1 values for which the Barkjohn
correction was originally calibrated~\cite{Barkjohn2021}. At the
ambient concentrations observed in Curitiba (median
4.12~µg/m$^3$), ALT and CF=1 readings converge~\cite{Holder2020},
but residual calibration uncertainty cannot be excluded.

Third, the analysis period (2022--2024) is relatively short, limiting
statistical power for rare events (e.g.\ cold spells, $n$=4) and
precluding long-term trend analysis.

Fourth, although the individual-level SIH data contain ICD-10 codes,
age, and sex, the primary ecological analysis used daily total counts
without subgroup stratification. The individual records support future
cause-specific analyses (e.g.\ pneumonia J09--J18, COPD J40--J47,
asthma J45--J46) and age-stratified models (0--4, 5--64, $\geq$65
years), which are a priority for subsequent work.

Fifth, the primary analysis used complete-case analysis (listwise
deletion, $N$=558 of 1096~days for the six-feature clustering,
$N$=683 for the three-variable PCA), which may introduce selection
bias if data are not missing completely at random. Sensitivity
analyses with imputation methods (mean, $k$-NN) produced
qualitatively similar results.

Sixth, the ecological analysis (PCA, clustering, lag correlations) did
not adjust for temporal confounders. While the machine learning models
included day-of-week and holiday features --- and SHAP confirmed these
as dominant predictors --- formal adjustment for influenza seasonality,
long-term trend, and other time-varying confounders requires
distributed lag non-linear models (DLNM) with quasi-Poisson
regression, which are a priority for future work.

Seventh, the ML forecasting models were trained and evaluated on a
single city with a relatively short time-series (3~years), limiting
external validity. The CMIP6 projections assume that the
exposure--response relationship remains stationary over decades, which
is a standard but potentially unrealistic assumption. Confidence
intervals for the projections were not estimated, and the Monte Carlo
simulations used simplified perturbation schemes rather than
physically consistent climate realisations.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This study demonstrates that in a subtropical metropolis, the
synergistic burden of cold extremes and particulate pollution on
respiratory hospital admissions exceeds that of heat alone. Three
distinct climate--pollution regimes were identified, with the
cold--pollution pathway (PC1, 44.5\% of variance) emerging as the
dominant driver of hospitalisations. The delayed lag structure (peak
at 9~days for temperature, 13~days for PM$_{2.5}$) suggests that
health effects accumulate over multi-week periods. Machine learning
models (XGBoost, MAPE=18--20\%) confirmed that day-of-week,
temperature, and PM$_{2.5}$ are the three most important predictors
of daily respiratory admissions, and CMIP6 SSP5-8.5 projections
suggest increasing hospitalisation burdens under continued warming
with a shift of peak vulnerability toward shoulder seasons.
Public health early-warning systems in the Southern Hemisphere should
integrate cold-spell forecasts with air-quality monitoring and
leverage operational ML-based hospitalisation forecasting. Future work
should extend this analysis to multiple cities, incorporate
cause-specific and age-stratified health outcomes, and apply formal
distributed lag non-linear models (DLNM) to quantify
exposure--response relationships.
