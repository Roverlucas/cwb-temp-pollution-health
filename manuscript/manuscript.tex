\title{Non-linear and delayed effects of cold extremes on respiratory
       hospital admissions in a subtropical metropolis: a quasi-Poisson
       time-series analysis in Curitiba, Brazil}

\begin{abstract}

\textbf{Background}\quad
The health effects of temperature extremes remain poorly characterised
in subtropical Southern Hemisphere cities, where cold spells produce
distinct exposure profiles that differ from those studied in temperate
and tropical settings.

\textbf{Methods}\quad
We analysed daily minimum temperature (INMET, 1961--2024) and
respiratory hospital admissions (DATASUS SIH/AIH, ICD-10 J00--J99,
2022--2024; $N$=42\,716) in Curitiba, Brazil ($\sim$1${\cdot}$8~million
inhabitants). Distributed lag non-linear models (DLNM) with
quasi-Poisson regression quantified non-linear, delayed
exposure--response associations over lags 0--21~days, adjusting for
day-of-week, holidays, and long-term trend via natural spline
(7~df/year). The minimum morbidity temperature (MMT) was identified
by grid search. Sensitivity analyses varied lag windows, seasonal
degrees of freedom, and excluded 2022 for residual COVID-19 effects.

\textbf{Findings}\quad
Over 1096~study days, 42\,716~respiratory hospitalisations (51\% male;
38\% aged $\geq$65~years) and 3715~in-hospital deaths were recorded.
The DLNM identified MMT=17${\cdot}$8\,°C. Cold extremes at the 1st
percentile of $T_\mathrm{min}$ (5${\cdot}$2\,°C) yielded cumulative
RR=1${\cdot}$42 [95\% CI: 1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84] over
0--21~days; at the 5th percentile (8${\cdot}$5\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$22
[1${\cdot}$02--1${\cdot}$44]. The lag structure peaked at 3--10~days.
Heat effects were imprecise, with wide confidence intervals
spanning the null (P90 RR=1${\cdot}$06
[0${\cdot}$98--1${\cdot}$14]; P99 RR=1${\cdot}$29
[0${\cdot}$98--1${\cdot}$68]). Results were robust across sensitivity
analyses ($\hat{\phi}$=1${\cdot}$32, Ljung--Box $p$=0${\cdot}$14).

\textbf{Interpretation}\quad
Cold extremes drive the clearest respiratory hospitalisation
burden in this subtropical setting, with delayed effects peaking at
3--10~days. Heat effects could not be precisely estimated given the
short series and few extreme hot days. Public health early-warning
systems should prioritise cold-spell forecasts alongside heat alerts.

None.

\end{abstract}

\section*{Research in Context}

\textbf{Evidence before this study}

We searched PubMed and Scopus for studies published between
January~2010 and December~2025 using the terms ``temperature
extremes'', ``hospital admissions'', ``distributed lag'', and
``Southern Hemisphere'' or ``Latin America''. Large multi-city studies
using DLNM methods have established U-shaped temperature--mortality
curves globally, but subtropical cities in the Southern Hemisphere
--- particularly in South America --- remain underrepresented. Few
studies have quantified the non-linear, delayed effects of cold
extremes on respiratory morbidity in this region.

\textbf{Added value of this study}

This study provides the first DLNM analysis of temperature extremes
and respiratory hospital admissions in a subtropical South American
city. Cold extremes at the 1st percentile of $T_\mathrm{min}$ are
associated with a 42\% increase in respiratory hospitalisations
(RR=1${\cdot}$42 [1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84]) with a delayed peak
at 3--10~days --- a lag structure not previously documented with
formal DLNM methods in this region.

\textbf{Implications of all the available evidence}

Public health early-warning systems in subtropical cities should
incorporate cold-spell forecasts alongside heat alerts, rather than
focusing exclusively on heat extremes. The delayed lag structure
(3--10~days) suggests a window for preventive intervention after
cold-spell onset.

\section{Introduction}

The relation between temperature extremes and human health has long
been recognised, with documented increases in mortality during
heatwaves across different
regions.\cite{Duthie1998,McMichael2006,Mestitz1959,Schuman1972}
Certain groups --- the elderly, children, and people with pre-existing
conditions --- are disproportionately
affected.\cite{Duthie1998,Lye1977,Schuman1972}

Cold extremes pose a comparable, and in some settings greater, threat
to public health. Low ambient temperatures trigger vasoconstriction,
elevate blood pressure, and increase blood viscosity, raising
cardiovascular risk.\cite{Ebi2021} Respiratory infections peak during
cold periods due to reduced mucociliary clearance and increased indoor
crowding.\cite{Linares2025} Unlike heat-related morbidity, which
manifests within 0--3~days, cold-related effects exhibit delayed lags
of 3--14~days, complicating attribution in ecological
studies.\cite{Cheng2024}

In the context of climate change, anthropogenic activities have
increased the frequency and intensity of extreme events and their
associated health impacts.\cite{Bell2024,Marx2021,McMichael2006}
Air pollutants affect respiratory, cardiovascular, and neural
systems,\cite{Linares2025,Sethi2024} and extreme pollution episodes
remain associated with increased hospital admissions and
mortality.\cite{Requia2024,Tseng2024}

Despite this evidence, few studies have quantified the non-linear,
delayed effects of temperature extremes on respiratory morbidity in
subtropical cities of the Southern Hemisphere using formal inferential
methods. Curitiba, a subtropical metropolis in southern Brazil
($\sim$930~m elevation, Cfb climate), offers a relevant case study
due to its winter temperatures below 5\,°C and seasonal pollution
episodes. This study uses distributed lag non-linear models to
quantify the exposure--response relationship between daily minimum
temperature and respiratory hospital admissions in Curitiba
(2022--2024), with supplementary clustering and machine learning
analyses.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Study design and setting}

This is an ecological time-series study examining the association
between daily environmental exposures and respiratory hospital
admissions in Curitiba, Paraná, Brazil (population~$\sim$1${\cdot}$8~million),
over 1~January~2022 to 31~December~2024 (1096~days). Curitiba's
subtropical climate features winter temperatures below 5\,°C and
summer maxima approaching 30\,°C.

\subsection{Data sources}

Individual-level hospital admission records were obtained from the
Brazilian Unified Health System (SIH/SUS--DATASUS) for 2022--2024
($N$=42\,716 respiratory admissions, ICD-10 J00--J99). The primary
outcome was the daily count of respiratory admissions. Demographics:
51\% male, 38\% aged $\geq$65~years. In-hospital mortality was
8${\cdot}$7\% ($n$=3715).

Daily minimum ($T_\mathrm{min}$), mean, and maximum temperatures,
relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure were obtained from INMET
automatic station~A807. For ETCCDI percentile calculations, the full
record (1961--2024) was used; combined analyses were restricted to
2022--2024.

PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ concentrations were measured using a PurpleAir
Flex-II sensor (Sensor ID~90875) at daily resolution. Raw readings
were corrected using the EPA nationwide correction
equation:\cite{Barkjohn2021}
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}^{\mathrm{corr}} =
    \text{0·524} \times \mathrm{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}^{\mathrm{sensor}}
    - \text{0·0862} \times \mathrm{RH} + \text{5·75}
  \label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
%
This correction has been validated against Federal Equivalent Method
monitors\cite{Holder2020} and is widely adopted for PurpleAir
data.\cite{Magi2020,Barkjohn2022} An inter-channel quality control
protocol excluded days when inter-channel differences exceeded
5~µg/m$^3$ and 70\% relative difference simultaneously. After QC,
697~days (63${\cdot}$6\%) had valid EPA-corrected PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$.

\subsection{Definitions and missing data}

Extreme temperature days were defined using ETCCDI monthly percentiles
(TX90p for extremely hot, TN10p for extremely cold) over the 1961--2024
baseline. Heatwaves: $\geq$3~consecutive extreme hot days; cold spells:
$\geq$3~consecutive extreme cold days. Missing data rates:
$T_\mathrm{min}$~2${\cdot}$6\%, PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$~36${\cdot}$4\%,
hospitalisations~0${\cdot}$2\%. Primary analyses used complete cases;
sensitivity analyses with imputation produced similar results
(Supplement).

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

Time-series were decomposed using STL to identify trend and seasonal
components. Augmented Dickey--Fuller (ADF) tests assessed stationarity.
PCA was performed on standardised PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$,
and hospitalisations ($N$=683 complete-case days). K-means clustering
was applied to six standardised variables, with optimal $K$ selected
by silhouette score. Cross-correlation functions (lags 0--28~days) were
computed between exposures and hospitalisations.

\subsection{Supplementary machine learning forecasting}

As a supplementary predictive analysis --- distinct from the
inferential DLNM framework above --- three models (XGBoost,\cite{Chen2016}
multilayer perceptron,\cite{Goodfellow2016} extreme learning
machine\cite{Huang2006}) were trained to forecast daily
hospitalisations at lags 0--7~days. SHAP values\cite{Lundberg2017}
quantified feature importance. These models do not estimate causal
effects but provide complementary evidence on variable relevance for
operational forecasting. Details, results, and CMIP6 projections
are reported in the Supplement (eMethods, eTable~8, eFigure~8).

\subsection{Distributed lag non-linear model}
\label{sec:dlnm}

We fitted distributed lag non-linear models
(DLNM)\cite{Gasparrini2010,Gasparrini2011} to quantify non-linear,
delayed exposure--response relationships:
%
\begin{equation}
  \log\!\big(\mathrm{E}[Y_t]\big) = \alpha
    + \mathbf{s}\bigl(x_t, \mathrm{lag}; \boldsymbol{\eta}\bigr)
    + \sum_{d=1}^{6} \beta_d \,\mathrm{DOW}_{d,t}
    + \gamma \,\mathrm{Holiday}_t
    + \mathrm{ns}(\mathrm{time}_t,\, 7\,\mathrm{df/year})
  \label{eq:dlnm}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_t$ is the daily count of respiratory admissions (quasi-Poisson),
$\mathbf{s}(\cdot)$ is the cross-basis function (natural cubic splines:
exposure df=4, lag df=5), DOW denotes day-of-week indicators, and
$\mathrm{ns}(\mathrm{time}, 21\,\mathrm{df})$ captures trend and
seasonality. Maximum lag was 21~days. The minimum morbidity temperature
(MMT) was identified by grid search, following
Gasparrini et al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} Confidence intervals were
computed via the delta method. Sensitivity analyses varied the maximum
lag (14, 21, 28~days), seasonal df (6, 7, 8~df/year), and excluded
2022 to assess residual COVID-19 effects.

\paragraph{Ethics and reporting}
This study used exclusively aggregated, publicly available data from
DATASUS, INMET, and PurpleAir. Under Brazilian CNS~510/2016,
research using public aggregated data is exempt from ethics review.
This study follows the STROBE guidelines.\cite{VonElm2007}

\section{Results}

\subsection{Descriptive statistics}

variables. A total of 42\,716~respiratory admissions and 3715~deaths
were recorded over 1096~days. Mean daily hospitalisations were
32${\cdot}$3 (SD~11${\cdot}$7, range 1--73). EPA-corrected
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ had a mean of 5${\cdot}$84~µg/m$^3$
(median~4${\cdot}$12), with 4${\cdot}$7\% of days exceeding the WHO
24-hour guideline (15~µg/m$^3$). We identified 215~extremely hot days
(20${\cdot}$1\%) and 39~extremely cold days (3${\cdot}$7\%),
aggregating into 25~heatwave and 4~cold-spell events (eTable~1).

\subsection{Exploratory analyses}

K-means clustering and lag-correlation analyses are reported in the
Supplement (eTables~2--5; eFigures~1--4) and should be interpreted
as exploratory pattern description rather than confirmatory evidence.
Briefly, three climate--pollution clusters emerged ($K$=3,
silhouette=0${\cdot}$357); however, the Polluted Heat cluster contained
only 12~days ($<$3\% of the sample), limiting its inferential value.
The Clean Cold cluster showed delayed positive correlations at
lags 3--10~days. Extended lag analysis revealed peak
$T_\mathrm{min}$--hospitalisation correlation at lag~9
($r$=$-$0${\cdot}$320, $p<$0${\cdot}$001), motivating the DLNM lag
window of 0--21~days.

\subsection{Distributed lag non-linear models}
\label{sec:dlnm_results}

\paragraph{Temperature exposure--response}
The quasi-Poisson DLNM for $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($N$=1068,
$\hat{\phi}$=1${\cdot}$32) identified MMT=17${\cdot}$8\,°C
(Figure~\ref{fig:dlnm_temp_overall}). Cold extremes showed
monotonically increasing cumulative RR below the MMT: at the 1st
percentile (5${\cdot}$2\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$42
[1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84]; at the 5th percentile (8${\cdot}$5\,°C),
RR=1${\cdot}$22 [1${\cdot}$02--1${\cdot}$44]; at the 10th percentile
(9${\cdot}$8\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$17 [1${\cdot}$00--1${\cdot}$37]
(Table~\ref{tab:dlnm_temp_rr}). Above the MMT, heat effects were imprecise: at the 90th
percentile (19${\cdot}$4\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$06
[0${\cdot}$98--1${\cdot}$14]; at the 99th percentile
(21${\cdot}$4\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$29 [0${\cdot}$98--1${\cdot}$68],
with wide confidence intervals that do not exclude a clinically
meaningful effect.
The lag structure at cold (P10) peaked at lags 3--10~days
(Figure~\ref{fig:dlnm_temp_lag}a), consistent with cross-correlation
results.

\paragraph{Sensitivity and diagnostics}
Results were robust to lag window and seasonal df
(Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}). Cumulative RR at P10 ranged from
1${\cdot}$05 (lag=14) to 1${\cdot}$23 (lag=28). Excluding 2022
strengthened the effect (RR=1${\cdot}$37 [1${\cdot}$11--1${\cdot}$70]).
Additional sensitivity analyses adjusting for relative humidity as a
linear covariate produced nearly identical temperature estimates
(RR=1${\cdot}$18 at P10, $N$=851), confirming that the cold effect is
not confounded by humidity variation. Models further adjusted for
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ showed attenuated estimates; however, this
attenuation is attributable to sample restriction bias, as
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$-complete days underrepresent cold temperatures
(eTable~9, eTable~10; see Limitations).
ADF on raw hospitalisations confirmed non-stationarity ($p$=0${\cdot}$164);
STL residuals were stationary ($p$=0${\cdot}$0003; eFigure~7).
Model diagnostics showed no autocorrelation (Durbin--Watson=2${\cdot}$00,
Ljung--Box $p$=0${\cdot}$14; eTable~7).

\subsection{Supplementary machine learning forecasting}

Supplementary predictive analyses (eTable~8, eFigure~8) showed
XGBoost as the best-performing forecasting model (MAPE=19${\cdot}$4\%
at lag~0, RMSE=8${\cdot}$37). SHAP analysis ranked temperature among
the most important predictors, providing complementary evidence
consistent with the DLNM findings above. These results support the
potential utility of ML-based early-warning systems but do not
substitute for the inferential estimates from the DLNM.

\section{Discussion}

Our DLNM analysis provides formal inferential evidence that cold
extremes are the dominant temperature-related driver of respiratory
hospitalisations in Curitiba. At the 1st percentile of
$T_\mathrm{min}$ (5${\cdot}$2\,°C), cumulative RR reached
1${\cdot}$42 [1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84] over 0--21~days, with a
delayed lag peak at 3--10~days. This lag structure is consistent with
known cold-related pathophysiology: cold-induced vasoconstriction,
elevated blood viscosity, and increased susceptibility to respiratory
infections manifest over days rather than
hours.\cite{Ebi2021,Linares2025}

These findings can be compared with the multi-country study by
Gasparrini et al.,\cite{Gasparrini2015} which reported U-shaped
temperature--mortality curves across 384~locations using the same
DLNM framework. Our MMT of 17${\cdot}$8\,°C is consistent with
subtropical MMTs reported in that study, and the cold-attributable
risk at P1 is comparable in magnitude to cold-related excess mortality
in temperate climates. Notably, Gasparrini et al. did not include
Curitiba or comparable Southern Brazilian cities, and our study fills
this gap by providing the first DLNM-based morbidity estimates for
this subtropical setting. However, as a single-city study, our results
lack the statistical strength of multi-site
pooling.\cite{Requia2024}

The sensitivity analyses support the robustness of the cold effect.
The direction and magnitude were consistent across lag windows
(14--28~days) and seasonal df (6--8/year). Excluding 2022
strengthened the effect (RR=1${\cdot}$37 [1${\cdot}$11--1${\cdot}$70]),
suggesting that residual COVID-19 effects may attenuate the
temperature--morbidity association in the full period.

Regarding heat effects, the point estimates above the MMT
(RR=1${\cdot}$06 at P90; RR=1${\cdot}$29 at P99) suggest a
possible J-shaped curve, but the wide confidence intervals preclude
firm conclusions. This imprecision likely reflects the short series
(three years) and the limited number of extreme hot days at the upper
tail of the Curitiba temperature distribution. We therefore interpret
the heat results as underpowered rather than null, and caution against
concluding that heat poses no respiratory risk in this
setting.\cite{Gasparrini2015}

These results have implications for public health early-warning
systems in subtropical settings. Cold-spell forecasts should be
prioritised alongside heat alerts, particularly given the delayed
lag structure (3--10~days) that provides a window for preventive
intervention.\cite{Bell2024}

\subsection{Limitations}

This ecological time-series study does not permit causal inference at
the individual level. No individual-level exposure, behaviour, or
comorbidity data were available. The 3-year analysis period
(2022--2024) limits power for rare events (cold spells, $n$=4) and
provides only three realisations of the seasonal cycle. However, the
primary DLNM estimates derive from the full continuous temperature
distribution ($N$=1068~days), not solely from classified extreme
events; sensitivity analyses varying lag windows and seasonal degrees
of freedom consistently reproduced the cold effect (Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}). PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ data had 36${\cdot}$4\% missingness due to sensor
downtime and inter-channel quality control. A comparison of key
variables between days with and without valid PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$
(eTable~10) revealed that days with missing PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ had
significantly lower $T_\mathrm{min}$ (13${\cdot}$8 vs 15${\cdot}$3\,°C,
$p<$0${\cdot}$001) and higher hospitalisations (34${\cdot}$8 vs
31${\cdot}$4/day, $p<$0${\cdot}$001), indicating that missingness
is missing at random (MAR) conditional on season/temperature rather
than MCAR. Consequently, models restricted to PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$-complete
days ($N$=683) underrepresent cold days and produce attenuated
temperature estimates (eTable~9). The humidity-only adjusted model,
which retained $N$=851~days, produced estimates nearly identical to
the unadjusted main model (RR=1${\cdot}$18 vs 1${\cdot}$17 at P10),
confirming robustness to humidity confounding. Future studies should
use reference-grade PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ monitors with higher data
completeness to address this limitation. Confounding
by respiratory viral circulation (influenza, RSV) cannot be excluded,
as viral surveillance data were not included. Daily total counts were
used without age or ICD-10 subgroup stratification; cause-specific
analyses are a priority for future work. Temperature data are from a
single INMET station (A807), which may not capture microclimatic
variation across the metropolitan area. As a single-city study,
results may not generalise; multi-city pooled analyses following
Gasparrini et al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} would provide stronger
evidence.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This study provides evidence that in a subtropical metropolis, cold
extremes are associated with increased respiratory hospitalisations,
with a delayed lag structure peaking at 3--10~days. The DLNM
identified MMT=17${\cdot}$8\,°C and estimated cumulative
RR=1${\cdot}$42 [1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84] at extreme cold (P1,
5${\cdot}$2\,°C) over 0--21~days. Heat effects were imprecise and
require longer observation periods to characterise.
Public health systems in subtropical Southern Hemisphere cities should
integrate cold-spell forecasts into early-warning systems. Future
work should extend this analysis to multiple cities with
cause-specific and age-stratified outcomes.
