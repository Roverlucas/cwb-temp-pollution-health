\begin{abstract}

\textbf{Background}\quad
The compound health effects of temperature extremes and particulate
air pollution remain poorly characterised in subtropical Southern
Hemisphere cities, where cold spells and boundary-layer inversions
produce distinct exposure profiles.

\textbf{Methods}\quad
We analysed daily minimum temperature (INMET, 1961--2024),
EPA-corrected PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ (PurpleAir, 2022--2024), and
respiratory hospital admissions (DATASUS SIH/AIH, ICD-10 J00--J99,
2022--2024; $N$=42\,716) in Curitiba, Brazil ($\sim$1${\cdot}$8~million
inhabitants). Extreme temperature days were defined using ETCCDI-based
monthly percentiles. Distributed lag non-linear models (DLNM) with
quasi-Poisson regression quantified non-linear, delayed
exposure--response associations (lag 0--21~days for temperature,
0--14~days for PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$), adjusting for day-of-week, holidays,
and long-term trend. The Relative Excess Risk due to Interaction (RERI)
assessed compound effects on the additive scale. K-means clustering
identified meteorological--pollution regimes. XGBoost forecasting with
SHAP interpretability was evaluated at lags 0--7~days. CMIP6 SSP5-8${\cdot}$5
projections estimated future hospitalisation burdens.

\textbf{Findings}\quad
Over 1096~study days, 42\,716~respiratory hospitalisations (51\% male;
38\% aged $\geq$65~years) and 3715~deaths were recorded. Three
climate--pollution clusters emerged: Clean Cold ($n$=248), Polluted
Heat ($n$=12, PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$=51${\cdot}$5~µg/m$^3$), and Clean Heat
($n$=298). The DLNM minimum morbidity temperature was 17${\cdot}$8\,°C.
Cold extremes at the 1st percentile of $T_\mathrm{min}$
(5${\cdot}$2\,°C) yielded cumulative RR=1${\cdot}$42
[95\% CI: 1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84] over 0--21~days, peaking at
lags 3--10. PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ showed weak effects at ambient
concentrations (median 4${\cdot}$1~µg/m$^3$). RERI=0${\cdot}$013
[$-$0${\cdot}$006, 0${\cdot}$033] was not significant, indicating
compound rather than supra-additive interaction. Model diagnostics
confirmed adequate fit ($\hat{\phi}$=1${\cdot}$32, Ljung--Box
$p$=0${\cdot}$14). XGBoost achieved MAPE=19${\cdot}$4\% at lag~0.

\textbf{Interpretation}\quad
Cold extremes, not heat, drive the greatest respiratory hospitalisation
burden in this subtropical setting, with delayed effects peaking at
3--10~days. Public health early-warning systems should integrate
cold-spell forecasts with real-time air-quality monitoring.

None.

\end{abstract}

\section*{Research in Context}

\textbf{Evidence before this study}

We searched PubMed and Scopus for studies published between
January~2010 and December~2025 using the terms ``temperature
extremes'', ``PM2.5'', ``hospital admissions'', and ``Southern
Hemisphere'' or ``Latin America''. While a growing body of evidence
links heat extremes and air pollution to mortality and morbidity ---
including large multi-city studies in Europe and East Asia --- few
studies have examined their combined or lagged effects in subtropical
cities of the Global South, where cold spells and atmospheric
inversions produce distinct compound exposure profiles.

\textbf{Added value of this study}

This study provides the first integrated analysis of temperature
extremes, particulate pollution, and hospital admissions in a
subtropical South American city using DLNM with quasi-Poisson
regression, K-means clustering, and machine learning forecasting.
We identified that cold extremes at the 1st percentile of
$T_\mathrm{min}$ are associated with a 42\% increase in respiratory
hospitalisations (RR=1${\cdot}$42 [1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84])
with a delayed response of 3--10~days --- a pattern not previously
documented with formal DLNM methods in this region.

\textbf{Implications of all the available evidence}

Public health early-warning systems in subtropical cities should
integrate cold-spell forecasts with air-quality monitoring, rather
than focusing exclusively on heat extremes. Climate-resilient health
planning must account for compound cold--pollution events as a
distinct and underappreciated risk pathway.

\section{Introduction}

The relation between temperature extremes and human health has long
been recognised, with documented increases in mortality during
heatwaves across different
regions.\cite{Duthie1998,McMichael2006,Mestitz1959,Schuman1972}
Certain groups --- the elderly, children, and people with pre-existing
conditions --- are disproportionately
affected.\cite{Duthie1998,Lye1977,Schuman1972}

Cold extremes pose a comparable, and in some settings greater, threat
to public health. Low ambient temperatures trigger vasoconstriction,
elevate blood pressure, and increase blood viscosity, raising
cardiovascular risk.\cite{Ebi2021} Respiratory infections peak during
cold periods due to reduced mucociliary clearance and increased indoor
crowding.\cite{Linares2025} Unlike heat-related morbidity, which
manifests within 0--3~days, cold-related effects exhibit delayed lags
of 3--14~days, complicating attribution in ecological
studies.\cite{Cheng2024}

In the context of climate change, anthropogenic activities have
increased the frequency and intensity of extreme events and their
associated health impacts.\cite{Bell2024,Marx2021,McMichael2006}
Air pollutants affect respiratory, cardiovascular, and neural
systems,\cite{Linares2025,Sethi2024} and extreme pollution episodes
remain associated with increased hospital admissions and
mortality.\cite{Requia2024,Tseng2024}

Temperature extremes and poor air quality share vulnerability profiles
and may interact. During cold periods, the shallow atmospheric
boundary layer reduces pollutant
dispersion,\cite{Linares2025,Xia2024} and the health effects of air
pollution often exhibit lagged
responses.\cite{Cheng2024,Pascal2021} Few studies have examined these
combined or lagged effects in the Southern Hemisphere. Curitiba, a
subtropical metropolis in southern Brazil, offers a relevant case study
due to its climatic variability and seasonal pollution episodes. This
study analyses the relationship between temperature, PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$,
and respiratory hospital admissions (2022--2024) using distributed lag
non-linear models, clustering, and machine learning forecasting.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Study design and setting}

This is an ecological time-series study examining the association
between daily environmental exposures and respiratory hospital
admissions in Curitiba, Paraná, Brazil (population~$\sim$1${\cdot}$8~million),
over 1~January~2022 to 31~December~2024 (1096~days). Curitiba's
subtropical climate features winter temperatures below 5\,°C and
summer maxima approaching 30\,°C.

\subsection{Data sources}

Individual-level hospital admission records were obtained from the
Brazilian Unified Health System (SIH/SUS--DATASUS) for 2022--2024
($N$=42\,716 respiratory admissions, ICD-10 J00--J99). The primary
outcome was the daily count of respiratory admissions. Demographics:
51\% male, 38\% aged $\geq$65~years. In-hospital mortality was
8${\cdot}$7\% ($n$=3715).

Daily minimum ($T_\mathrm{min}$), mean, and maximum temperatures,
relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure were obtained from INMET
automatic station~A807. For ETCCDI percentile calculations, the full
record (1961--2024) was used; combined analyses were restricted to
2022--2024.

PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ concentrations were measured using a PurpleAir
Flex-II sensor (Sensor ID~90875) at daily resolution. Raw readings
were corrected using the EPA nationwide correction
equation:\cite{Barkjohn2021}
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}^{\mathrm{corr}} =
    \text{0·524} \times \mathrm{PM}_{2{\cdot}5}^{\mathrm{sensor}}
    - \text{0·0862} \times \mathrm{RH} + \text{5·75}
  \label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
%
This correction has been validated against Federal Equivalent Method
monitors\cite{Holder2020} and is widely adopted for PurpleAir
data.\cite{Magi2020,Barkjohn2022} An inter-channel quality control
protocol excluded days when inter-channel differences exceeded
5~µg/m$^3$ and 70\% relative difference simultaneously. After QC,
697~days (63${\cdot}$6\%) had valid EPA-corrected PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$.

\subsection{Definitions and missing data}

Extreme temperature days were defined using ETCCDI monthly percentiles
(TX90p for extremely hot, TN10p for extremely cold) over the 1961--2024
baseline. Heatwaves: $\geq$3~consecutive extreme hot days; cold spells:
$\geq$3~consecutive extreme cold days. Missing data rates:
$T_\mathrm{min}$~2${\cdot}$6\%, PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$~36${\cdot}$4\%,
hospitalisations~0${\cdot}$2\%. Primary analyses used complete cases;
sensitivity analyses with imputation produced similar results
(Supplement).

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

Time-series were decomposed using STL to identify trend and seasonal
components. Augmented Dickey--Fuller (ADF) tests assessed stationarity.
PCA was performed on standardised PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$,
and hospitalisations ($N$=683 complete-case days). K-means clustering
was applied to six standardised variables, with optimal $K$ selected
by silhouette score. Cross-correlation functions (lags 0--28~days) were
computed between exposures and hospitalisations.

\subsection{Machine learning forecasting}

Three models --- XGBoost,\cite{Chen2016} multilayer
perceptron,\cite{Goodfellow2016} and extreme learning
machine\cite{Huang2006} --- were trained to forecast daily
hospitalisations at lags 0--7~days using PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$, temperature,
humidity, day-of-week, and holiday features. SHAP values\cite{Lundberg2017}
quantified feature importance. CMIP6 SSP5-8${\cdot}$5 projections
estimated future hospitalisations (details in Supplement).

\subsection{Distributed lag non-linear model}
\label{sec:dlnm}

We fitted distributed lag non-linear models
(DLNM)\cite{Gasparrini2010,Gasparrini2011} to quantify non-linear,
delayed exposure--response relationships:
%
\begin{equation}
  \log\!\big(\mathrm{E}[Y_t]\big) = \alpha
    + \mathbf{s}\bigl(x_t, \mathrm{lag}; \boldsymbol{\eta}\bigr)
    + \sum_{d=1}^{6} \beta_d \,\mathrm{DOW}_{d,t}
    + \gamma \,\mathrm{Holiday}_t
    + \mathrm{ns}(\mathrm{time}_t,\, 7\,\mathrm{df/year})
  \label{eq:dlnm}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_t$ is the daily count of respiratory admissions (quasi-Poisson),
$\mathbf{s}(\cdot)$ is the cross-basis function (natural cubic splines:
exposure df=4, lag df=5), DOW denotes day-of-week indicators, and
$\mathrm{ns}(\mathrm{time}, 21\,\mathrm{df})$ captures trend and
seasonality. Maximum lag was 21~days for temperature and 14~days for
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$. The minimum morbidity temperature (MMT) was
identified by grid search, following
Gasparrini et al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} Confidence intervals were
computed via the delta method.

A joint model assessed interaction via the Relative Excess Risk due
to Interaction (RERI) on the additive scale,\cite{Cheng2024} with
RERI~$>$~0 and 95\% CI excluding zero indicating supra-additive
interaction. Sensitivity analyses varied the maximum lag (14, 21,
28~days), seasonal df (6, 7, 8~df/year), and excluded 2022 to assess
residual COVID effects. Benjamini--Hochberg
FDR correction was applied.

\paragraph{Ethics and reporting}
This study used exclusively aggregated, publicly available data from
DATASUS, INMET, and PurpleAir. Under Brazilian CNS~510/2016,
research using public aggregated data is exempt from ethics review.
This study follows the STROBE guidelines.\cite{VonElm2007}

\subsection{Clustering and lag-correlation}

Three climate--pollution clusters emerged ($K$=3, silhouette=0${\cdot}$357,
$N$=558): Clean Cold ($n$=248, PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$=4${\cdot}$8~µg/m$^3$),
Polluted Heat ($n$=12, PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$=51${\cdot}$5~µg/m$^3$,
38${\cdot}$6~hospitalisations/day), and Clean Heat ($n$=298,
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$=4${\cdot}$9~µg/m$^3$); Kruskal--Wallis $p$=0${\cdot}$039
(eTables~2--3; eFigures~1--3). Clean Cold showed delayed positive
correlations at lags 3--10~days; Polluted Heat showed immediate
response (lags 0--4; eFigure~4). Extended lag analysis revealed peak
$T_\mathrm{min}$--hospitalisation correlation at lag~9
($r$=$-$0${\cdot}$320, $p<$0${\cdot}$001) persisting through lag~28
(eTable~5). PCA identified a cold--pollution component (PC1,
44${\cdot}$5\% variance) associating cold, polluted days with higher
admissions (eTable~4).

\subsection{Distributed lag non-linear models}
\label{sec:dlnm_results}

\paragraph{Temperature exposure--response}
The quasi-Poisson DLNM for $T_\mathrm{min}$ ($N$=1068,
$\hat{\phi}$=1${\cdot}$32) identified MMT=17${\cdot}$8\,°C
(Figure~\ref{fig:dlnm_temp_overall}). Cold extremes showed
monotonically increasing cumulative RR below the MMT: at the 1st
percentile (5${\cdot}$2\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$42
[1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84]; at the 5th percentile (8${\cdot}$5\,°C),
RR=1${\cdot}$22 [1${\cdot}$02--1${\cdot}$44]; at the 10th percentile
(9${\cdot}$8\,°C), RR=1${\cdot}$17 [1${\cdot}$00--1${\cdot}$37]
(Table~\ref{tab:dlnm_temp_rr}). Heat effects were non-significant at
the 90th percentile (RR=1${\cdot}$06 [0${\cdot}$98--1${\cdot}$14]).
The lag structure at cold (P10) peaked at lags 3--10~days
(Figure~\ref{fig:dlnm_temp_lag}a), consistent with cross-correlation
results.

\paragraph{PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ and interaction}
The PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ DLNM ($N$=697, $\hat{\phi}$=1${\cdot}$33) showed
weak cumulative effects at ambient concentrations: RR=0${\cdot}$91
[0${\cdot}$78--1${\cdot}$07] at P90 (10${\cdot}$2~µg/m$^3$;
eFigure~6). The joint model ($N$=683) yielded RERI=0${\cdot}$013
[$-$0${\cdot}$006, 0${\cdot}$033], not significant, indicating
compound rather than supra-additive effects (AP=0${\cdot}$019,
S=0${\cdot}$955; eTable~6).

\paragraph{Sensitivity and diagnostics}
Results were robust to lag window and seasonal df
(Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}). Cumulative RR at P10 ranged from
1${\cdot}$05 (lag=14) to 1${\cdot}$23 (lag=28). Excluding 2022
strengthened the effect (RR=1${\cdot}$37 [1${\cdot}$11--1${\cdot}$70]).
ADF on raw hospitalisations confirmed non-stationarity ($p$=0${\cdot}$164);
STL residuals were stationary ($p$=0${\cdot}$0003; eFigure~7).
Model diagnostics showed no autocorrelation (Durbin--Watson=2${\cdot}$00,
Ljung--Box $p$=0${\cdot}$14; eTable~7).

\subsection{Machine learning forecasting}

XGBoost was the best-performing model (MAPE=19${\cdot}$4\% at lag~0,
RMSE=8${\cdot}$37; eTable~8). Performance was stable across lags
0--7~days. SHAP analysis ranked day-of-week, temperature, and
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ as the top three predictors (eFigure~8). Under CMIP6
SSP5-8${\cdot}$5 projections, mean temperature increases of
$\sim$1${\cdot}$2\,°C by 2050 shifted peak vulnerability from winter
to the shoulder seasons, with Monte Carlo cold-wave simulations
producing disproportionate hospitalisation increases during
autumn/spring transitions.

\section{Discussion}

Our analysis suggests that the strongest compound health effects in
Curitiba arise from cold--pollution events, not heat alone. The DLNM
results provide formal inferential support: cold extremes at the 1st
percentile of $T_\mathrm{min}$ (5${\cdot}$2\,°C) were associated
with a 42\% increase in respiratory hospitalisations (RR=1${\cdot}$42
[1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84]) over 0--21~days, with a delayed lag
peak at 3--10~days. This is consistent with the shallow
boundary-layer mechanism, whereby cold stable conditions trap
pollutants near the surface.\cite{Linares2025,Xia2024}

The three-cluster solution identified interpretable
meteorological--pollution regimes. The delayed lag pattern for cold
(peak at lags 3--10) contrasts with the immediate response to extreme
pollution (lags 0--4), suggesting distinct pathophysiological
pathways. Cold-induced vasoconstriction and increased susceptibility
to respiratory infections may explain the longer
lag,\cite{Ebi2021} while acute particulate exposure triggers rapid
airway inflammation.\cite{Cheng2024} The Polluted Heat cluster
averaged 38${\cdot}$6~hospitalisations/day despite comprising only
12~days (2\% of the sample).

The non-significant RERI (0${\cdot}$013 [$-$0${\cdot}$006,
0${\cdot}$033]) indicates that temperature and PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ act
through compound rather than supra-additive pathways, consistent with
parallel mechanistic routes.\cite{Pascal2021} The PCA results
reinforce this: PC1 (44${\cdot}$5\% variance) loads positively on
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ and hospitalisations and negatively on
$T_\mathrm{min}$, confirming that cold, polluted days drive
admissions.

These findings can be compared with the multi-country study by
Gasparrini et al.,\cite{Gasparrini2015} which reported U-shaped
temperature--mortality curves across 384~locations using the same
DLNM framework. Our MMT of 17${\cdot}$8\,°C is consistent with
subtropical MMTs in that study, and the cold-attributable risk
(RR=1${\cdot}$42 at P1) is comparable in magnitude to cold-related
excess mortality in temperate climates. However, as a single-city
morbidity study, our results lack the statistical strength of
multi-site pooling. In São Paulo, winter inversions have been linked
to increased respiratory hospitalisations, though the cold--pollution
pathway was not explicitly modelled.\cite{Requia2024}

The SHAP analysis quantified what the correlation analysis suggested:
after the dominant day-of-week effect, temperature and PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$
are the second and third most important predictors. XGBoost achieved
MAPE of 18--20\% across lags, comparable to similar environmental
health forecasting studies.\cite{Chen2016} CMIP6 projections suggest
that warming will not eliminate the cold--health burden but may shift
peak vulnerability to the shoulder seasons.

These results have implications for Climate Early Warning Systems.
Cold-spell forecasts should be integrated with real-time
PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$ monitoring to identify compound events, which pose
a greater hospitalisation risk in subtropical settings than heat
alone.\cite{Bell2024} The ML models could serve as the core of
operational 1--7-day-ahead hospitalisation forecasting tools.

\subsection{Limitations}

This ecological time-series study does not permit causal inference at
the individual level. No individual-level exposure, behaviour, or
comorbidity data were available. The single low-cost PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$
sensor introduces measurement uncertainty; although the EPA
correction\cite{Barkjohn2021} was applied, the archived data contained
ALT-corrected readings rather than raw CF=1 values, and 36${\cdot}$4\%
of days lacked valid PM$_{2{\cdot}5}$. The 3-year analysis period
limits power for rare events (cold spells, $n$=4). Daily total counts
were used without age or diagnosis stratification; cause-specific
analyses are a priority for future work. Complete-case analysis may
introduce selection bias if data are not missing completely at random.
As a single-city study, results may not generalise; multi-city pooled
analyses following Gasparrini et al.\cite{Gasparrini2015} would
provide stronger evidence. ML models were trained on a short
time-series with limited external validity, and CMIP6 projections
assume stationarity of the exposure--response relationship.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This study provides evidence that in a subtropical metropolis, the
compound burden of cold extremes and particulate pollution on
respiratory admissions exceeds that of heat alone. The DLNM identified
MMT=17${\cdot}$8\,°C and estimated cumulative RR=1${\cdot}$42
[1${\cdot}$09--1${\cdot}$84] at extreme cold (P1, 5${\cdot}$2\,°C)
over 0--21~days. The RERI analysis indicated compound rather than
supra-additive interaction. Three climate--pollution regimes were
identified, with the cold--pollution pathway emerging as the dominant
driver. Public health systems in the Southern Hemisphere should
integrate cold-spell forecasts with air-quality monitoring and
leverage ML-based hospitalisation forecasting. Future work should
extend this analysis to multiple cities with cause-specific and
age-stratified outcomes.
