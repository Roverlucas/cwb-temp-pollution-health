The combined health effects of temperature extremes and particulate
air pollution remain poorly characterised in subtropical cities of the
Southern Hemisphere, where cold spells and boundary-layer inversions
can produce compound exposure events.

\section*{Research in Context}

\textbf{Evidence before this study}

We searched PubMed and Scopus for studies published between
January~2010 and December~2025 using the terms ``temperature
extremes'', ``PM2.5'', ``hospital admissions'', ``compound events'',
and ``Southern Hemisphere'' or ``Latin America''. While a growing body
of evidence links heat extremes and air pollution to mortality and
morbidity in temperate and tropical regions, few studies have examined
their combined or lagged effects in subtropical cities of the Global
South, where cold spells and atmospheric inversions produce distinct
compound exposure profiles.
\TODO{Refinar busca e citar números de artigos encontrados.}

\textbf{Added value of this study}

This study provides the first integrated analysis of temperature
extremes, particulate pollution, and hospital admissions in a large
subtropical South American city using daily data spanning 2021--2024.
By combining PCA, clustering, and lag-correlation analysis, we
identified four recurrent climate--pollution regimes and demonstrated
that the strongest associations with hospitalisations occur during
cold, polluted days with a delayed response of 3--10~days --- a pattern
not previously documented in this region.

\textbf{Implications of all the available evidence}

Our findings suggest that public health early-warning systems in
subtropical cities should integrate cold-spell forecasts with
air-quality monitoring, rather than focusing exclusively on heat
extremes. Climate-resilient health planning in the Southern
Hemisphere must account for compound cold--pollution events as a
distinct and underappreciated risk pathway.

\section{Introduction}

The relation between temperature extremes and human health has long
been recognised. Historical records report increased mortality during
heatwaves across different regions of the
world~\cite{Duthie1998,McMichael2006,Mestitz1959,Poumadere2005,Schuman1972,Trenberth2012}.
As early as 1959, the acute effects of elevated temperatures on
health were described as recently observed, with earlier mentions in
non-scientific literature~\cite{Mestitz1959}. Since then, many
reports have aimed to describe the phenomenon and its
statistics~\cite{Kalkstein1995,Katsouyanni1988,Schuman1972} and
investigate the cause--effect relationship between heat and
mortality~\cite{Duthie1998,Lye1977,McMichael2006}. A broad consensus
is that certain groups --- the elderly, children, pregnant women, and
people with pre-existing conditions --- are disproportionately
affected~\cite{Duthie1998,Lye1977,Schuman1972}.

\TODO{Escrever parágrafo equivalente para coldwaves --- placeholder no .docx original.}

In the context of climate change, although heatwaves, cold spells,
and other extreme events can occur naturally, anthropogenic activities
have been increasing their frequency and
intensity~\cite{Bell2024,Marx2021,McMichael2006}. Consequently, their
associated health impacts have also
intensified~\cite{TheLancet2018}. In recent years, episodes of
intense heatwaves in Europe, India, China, the USA, and Saudi Arabia
have drawn heightened attention from the scientific
community~\cite{Marx2021,Nashwan2024,Rowland2021,TheLancet2022}.

Since the industrial revolution, air pollutant emissions have been a
main driver of anthropogenic climate
change~\cite{Beevers2025,McMichael2006}. Different gaseous and
particulate pollutants directly affect respiratory, cardiovascular,
and neural systems~\cite{Linares2025,Sethi2024}. Despite
improvements in air quality in some parts of the
world~\cite{Gong2025,Gopikrishnan2025}, extreme pollution episodes
remain associated with increased hospital admissions, morbidity, and
mortality~\cite{Gardasevic2024,Hertzog2024,Requia2024,Tseng2024}.

Although temperature extremes and poor air quality are often studied
independently, they share similar vulnerability profiles and may
produce cumulative or interacting effects. Days with the worst air
quality tend to occur during colder periods when the atmospheric
boundary layer is shallower, reducing the vertical dispersion of
pollutants~\cite{Linares2025,Xia2024}. Moreover, the health effects
of air pollution often exhibit a lagged response, emerging days or
weeks after exposure~\cite{Cheng2024,Pascal2021}.

Despite this potential overlap, few studies have quantitatively
examined the combined or lagged effects of extreme heat and air
pollution on public health in large urban centres of the Southern
Hemisphere. Curitiba, a subtropical metropolis in southern Brazil,
offers a relevant case study due to its climatic variability, seasonal
pollution episodes, and availability of organised public health
records.

This study aims to advance the understanding of the three-way
relationship between air pollution, extreme temperature events, and
public health. Time-series of surface air temperature, particulate
matter (PM$_{2.5}$), and hospital admissions in Curitiba (2021--2024)
were analysed. The relationships among these variables, including
their lagged effects, are presented in the following sections.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Study area and population}

Three datasets were required: hospitalisation records, atmospheric
pollution measurements, and surface air temperature. Brazil's
national public health system (SUS) provides daily hospitalisation
counts by municipality through the DATASUS information system. The
city of Curitiba, capital of Paraná state, was selected as the study
site.

Curitiba is located in the southern region of Brazil and has
approximately 1.8~million inhabitants, comparable to cities such as
Warsaw, Vienna, and Philadelphia. Its climate is influenced by both
subtropical and temperate meteorological systems (cold fronts, polar
air masses, and dry spells). Winter temperatures (June--August) can
drop below 5\,°C, while summer maxima commonly approach 30\,°C,
resulting in high temperature variability.

Temperature data were obtained from a meteorological station located
at $-25.45$, $-49.23$. An atmospheric pollution sensor (PurpleAir
Flex-II) was co-located at this site to ensure spatial alignment
between pollution and meteorological measurements.

Total daily hospital admissions within the territory of Curitiba were
obtained from DATASUS for the period 2021--2024.

\subsection{Definitions}

Extreme temperature days were defined using monthly percentiles
computed over the full INMET record. Days where $T_\mathrm{max}$
exceeded the monthly 90th percentile ($Q_{90}$) were classified as
extremely hot; days where $T_\mathrm{min}$ fell below the monthly
10th percentile ($Q_{10}$) were classified as extremely cold.
Heatwaves and cold spells were defined as $\geq$3 consecutive extreme
days.

\subsection{Missing data}
\label{sec:missing}

Missing values were present in all three datasets. In the complete
analysis dataset (2022--2024, $N$=1096~days), the proportion of
missing values was: $T_\mathrm{max}$~2.4\%, $T_\mathrm{med}$~4.9\%,
$T_\mathrm{min}$~2.6\%, PM$_{2.5}$~15.1\%, relative
humidity~21.6\%, and pressure~15.1\%. Complete cases across all six
clustering features totalled 773~days (70.5\%). The primary analysis
used listwise deletion (complete cases only); sensitivity analyses
with mean imputation and $k$-nearest-neighbour imputation ($k$=5)
are reported in the Supplement.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

\paragraph{Exploratory analysis.}
Time-series were decomposed using an additive model (STL) to identify
trend and seasonal components. Autocorrelation functions (ACF) and
Augmented Dickey--Fuller (ADF) tests were used to assess
stationarity. Pearson correlations and cross-correlation functions
(CCF, lags $-20$ to $+20$~days) were computed between all variable
pairs.

\paragraph{Principal component analysis.}
PCA was performed on the standardised daily PM$_{2.5}$,
$T_\mathrm{min}$, and hospitalisation series. The first two
components, explaining 73.7\% of total variance, were retained.

\paragraph{Clustering.}
K-means clustering was applied to six standardised variables
($T_\mathrm{max}$, $T_\mathrm{med}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$,
PM$_{2.5}$, relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure). Optimal
cluster number was selected by maximising the silhouette score over
$K$=2--10 (optimal $K$=3; manuscript validation also performed at
$K$=4). Method comparison included Ward hierarchical clustering and
DBSCAN. The full clustering pipeline is implemented in
\texttt{analysis/clustering/cluster\_analysis.py}.

\paragraph{Health lag-correlation analysis.}
Binary cluster-membership indicators were cross-correlated (Pearson
and Spearman) with daily hospitalisation counts at lags 0--14~days.
Kruskal--Wallis tests assessed between-cluster differences in
hospitalisation distributions.

Three algorithms --- Extreme Learning Machine (ELM), Multilayer
Perceptron (MLP), and XGBoost --- were trained to predict daily
hospitalisations from meteorological and air-quality inputs.

The temperature time-series (1961--2024) showed a rising trend. Mean
daily temperature increased from 16.71\,°C (1961--1985) to
18.20\,°C (2007--2024), a rise of 1.42\,°C. Hospitalisations in
Curitiba also showed an increasing trend, even after adjusting for

Cross-correlation analysis yielded weak linear associations (all
$|r|<0.30$), motivating the use of multivariate methods (PCA,
clustering).

\subsection{Lag-correlation with hospitalisations}

Cluster-membership analysis revealed distinct lag patterns
(Figure~\ref{fig:lag}). Cluster~0 (polluted heat) showed the
strongest positive correlations with hospitalisations at lags of 0
and 4~days, suggesting an immediate and short-delayed response to hot
and polluted conditions. Cluster~1 (clean cold) showed increasing
correlations from lag~3 to lag~10, indicative of a delayed and
prolonged response to cold exposure. Cluster~2 (hot, clean air)
showed exclusively negative correlations across all lags, suggesting
a protective effect of good air quality even under thermal stress.

Kruskal--Wallis tests confirmed significant between-cluster
differences in daily hospitalisations ($H$=49.01, $p<0.0001$ at
$K$=4).

\section{Discussion}

Our analysis of daily temperature, PM$_{2.5}$, and hospitalisation
data in Curitiba revealed that the strongest compound health effects
arise from cold--pollution events, not heat alone. This finding is
consistent with the shallow boundary-layer mechanism, whereby cold
stable atmospheric conditions trap pollutants near the surface,
producing a ``double hit'' of cold stress and elevated PM$_{2.5}$
exposure~\cite{Linares2025,Xia2024}.

The four-cluster solution identified interpretable
meteorological--pollution regimes. The delayed lag pattern observed
for cold days (peak correlation at 3--10~days) contrasts with the
more immediate response to hot, polluted conditions (lag 0--4~days),
suggesting distinct pathophysiological pathways. Cold-induced
vasoconstriction and increased susceptibility to respiratory
infections may explain the longer lag~\cite{Ebi2021}.

The exclusively negative correlations observed for hot days with
clean air (Cluster~2) are noteworthy, suggesting that temperature
alone is insufficient to drive hospitalisations when air quality is
good. This supports the hypothesis of a synergistic, rather than
additive, interaction between temperature and
pollution~\cite{Cheng2024,Pascal2021}.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study has several limitations. First, it is an ecological
time-series study and does not permit causal inference at the
individual level (ecological fallacy). Second, the use of a single
low-cost PM$_{2.5}$ sensor introduces measurement uncertainty,
although the PurpleAir Flex-II has been validated against reference
monitors in previous studies. Third, the analysis period (2021--2024)
is relatively short and overlaps with the COVID-19 pandemic, which
may have altered both baseline hospitalisation rates and air-quality
patterns. Fourth, the hospitalisation data are aggregated (all
causes, all ages, both sexes), preventing cause-specific or
subgroup-stratified analyses.

The principal analysis used complete-case analysis (listwise deletion,
$N$=773 of 1096~days), which may introduce selection bias. Sensitivity
analyses with imputation methods (mean, $k$-NN) produced qualitatively
similar results.

Finally, the climate projections rely on XGBoost trained on
observational data and applied to CMIP6 outputs, which assumes
stationarity of exposure--response relationships --- an assumption
that may not hold under future climate regimes.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This study demonstrates that in a subtropical metropolis, the
synergistic burden of cold extremes and particulate pollution on
hospital admissions exceeds that of heat alone. Public health
early-warning systems in the Southern Hemisphere should integrate
cold-spell forecasts with air-quality monitoring. Climate-resilient
health planning must account for compound cold--pollution events as a
distinct and underappreciated risk pathway. Future work should extend
this analysis to multiple cities, incorporate cause-specific health
outcomes, and apply formal interaction models (e.g.\ DLNM) to
quantify exposure--response relationships.
